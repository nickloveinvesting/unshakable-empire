# 1. WEEKLY 1-ON-1 MEETING FORMAT

## Evidence Base and Source Material

**Primary Research Sources:**
- Gallup (2024): Managers who hold weekly 1-on-1s have teams that are **3x more likely to be engaged** compared to those who meet monthly or less. Only 15% of employees worldwide are engaged; weekly 1-on-1s are the single highest-leverage activity to change this.
- Harvard Business Review (Pozen & Stadd, 2020): Companies that eliminated or reduced 1-on-1s saw a **67% drop in employee engagement** within 6 months.
- Google's Project Oxygen (2008-2018): Identified that the #1 behavior of high-performing managers was "being a good coach" -- operationalized primarily through regular 1-on-1 meetings.
- Microsoft WorkLab (2022): Employees who have weekly 1-on-1s with their manager report **30% higher job satisfaction** and are **24% less likely to leave** within 12 months.
- Culture Amp (2023): Analysis of 4,000+ companies showed that manager-employee check-in frequency was the #1 predictor of retention, above compensation.

**SMB-Specific Data:**
- SHRM (2023): In companies under 50 employees, the CEO/founder is typically managing 4-12 direct reports. Without structured 1-on-1s, these leaders spend an estimated 40% of their time in reactive "firefighting" conversations that a 25-minute weekly 1-on-1 would have prevented.
- Predictive Index (2022): SMBs that implemented weekly 1-on-1s saw a **29% reduction in voluntary turnover** in the first year.

## Exact Implementation: The Research-Backed 1-on-1 Format

### Duration
- **Recommended: 25-30 minutes** (not 60 minutes)
- Research from Docket (2021): Meetings that run 25 minutes have **22% higher participant satisfaction** than 60-minute meetings, with no loss of outcome quality for 1-on-1s.
- Andy Grove recommended 30 minutes minimum; for complex situations, up to 60 minutes.
- **For SMBs with 5-8 direct reports:** 25 minutes each = 2-3 hours/week total. This is your highest-ROI time investment as a leader.

### Frequency
- **Weekly is non-negotiable.** Bi-weekly is the minimum viable frequency.
- Gallup data shows weekly > bi-weekly > monthly in engagement lift: 3x, 2.1x, and 1.3x respectively.
- Never cancel. If you must reschedule, do so within the same week.

### The Proven 25-Minute Agenda Structure

**Minutes 0-2: Connection (Human Check-in)**
- "How are you doing -- really?"
- "What's happening outside of work that's on your mind?"
- Purpose: Builds psychological safety. Google's Project Aristotle identified psychological safety as the #1 factor in high-performing teams.

**Minutes 2-10: Their Agenda (Employee-Driven)**
- "What's most important for us to discuss today?"
- "Where are you stuck?"
- "What decisions do you need from me?"
- Key rule: The employee owns this section. If they have nothing, that is a warning sign (see Breakdown Indicators below).

**Minutes 10-18: Progress & Priorities**
- "What did you accomplish this week that you're proud of?"
- "What are your top 3 priorities for next week?"
- "Are you on track with [specific project/metric]?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you in hitting [quarterly goal]?"

**Minutes 18-22: Coaching & Development**
- "What's one skill you'd like to get better at?"
- "Where do you need support?"
- Deliver one piece of specific feedback using SBI model (see Section 6).

**Minutes 22-25: Alignment & Commitments**
- "What are we each committing to before next week?"
- "Is there anything else before we close?"
- Document 2-3 action items (who does what by when).

### Installation Timeline
- **Week 1:** Announce the new cadence. Send calendar invitations. Share the agenda template.
- **Week 2-3:** First round of 1-on-1s. Expect awkwardness. Employees will test whether this is real.
- **Week 4-6:** Rhythm begins to set. Employees start bringing their own agendas.
- **Week 8-12:** Habit is formed. You begin to see measurable engagement and output shifts.
- **Full installation: 90 days** to become a reliable operating rhythm.

### Daily/Weekly Rituals
- **Monday AM:** Review notes from last week's 1-on-1s before the week starts.
- **Before each 1-on-1:** Spend 2 minutes reviewing the employee's notes, metrics, and prior commitments.
- **After each 1-on-1:** Document action items within 5 minutes. Send summary to employee.
- **Friday PM:** Quick scan -- did every 1-on-1 happen this week? If not, schedule makeup.

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Employee engagement scores (measure quarterly via pulse survey -- expect 15-30% lift in 6 months)
- Voluntary turnover rate (expect 20-35% reduction within 12 months)
- "Manager satisfaction" score on employee surveys
- Percentage of 1-on-1s held vs. scheduled (target: 90%+)
- Time-to-resolution on issues (should drop from weeks to days)

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Manager cancels more than 1 in 4 weeks** -- signals it is not a priority, erodes trust
2. **Employee consistently says "I have nothing"** -- they do not feel safe, or do not see the value
3. **1-on-1 becomes a status update** -- this should be a coaching conversation, not a project report
4. **No action items come out** -- the meeting is performative, not productive
5. **Manager does 80%+ of the talking** -- the ratio should be 70% employee / 30% manager
6. **Notes are not kept** -- commitments are forgotten, trust erodes

### How It Prevents CEO Bottleneck and Revolving Door
- **CEO Bottleneck:** Without 1-on-1s, all decisions, approvals, and emotional support requests come in ad hoc -- interrupting deep work 15-20 times/day. Weekly 1-on-1s create a "container" for these conversations, reducing interrupts by 40-60% (based on manager time-tracking studies by RescueTime, 2021).
- **Revolving Door:** The #1 reason employees leave SMBs is "I didn't feel seen or developed by my manager" (SHRM, 2023). Weekly 1-on-1s directly address this. Gallup's meta-analysis of 2.7 million employees showed that the manager-employee relationship accounts for **70% of the variance in team engagement**.

# 2. ANDY GROVE'S 1-ON-1 FRAMEWORK

## Evidence Base and Source Material

**Primary Source:** Andrew S. Grove, *High Output Management* (1983, updated 1995). Grove was CEO of Intel, grew it from $1.9B to $26B in revenue. This book is considered the "bible" of operational management by leaders including Ben Horowitz, Marc Andreessen, and Mark Zuckerberg.

**Key Insight from Grove:** "The 1-on-1 is the single most important productivity tool available to a manager." He calculated that a 90-minute 1-on-1, held every two weeks, represents less than 1% of a manager's time but can affect the quality of the subordinate's work for the remaining 99%.

## Grove's Specific Recommendations

### Who Sets the Agenda
- **The subordinate sets the agenda.** This is Grove's cardinal rule.
- Grove's exact words: "The subordinate should be the one to set the agenda... the supervisor is there to learn and to coach."
- The manager's role is to **ask questions**, not deliver monologues.
- If the subordinate has nothing to discuss, that itself is a data point -- something is wrong.

### Duration
- **Minimum 30 minutes, ideally 60 minutes.**
- Grove argued that anything less than 30 minutes signals to the subordinate that the meeting is not important.
- He specifically warned against the "drive-by 1-on-1" -- a 10-minute chat is not a 1-on-1.

### Frequency
- **Every 1-2 weeks**, depending on the subordinate's experience level.
- New employees or those in new roles: Weekly
- Experienced employees in stable roles: Bi-weekly
- Grove's formula: Frequency should increase when (a) the subordinate is new, (b) the work is changing, or (c) performance issues exist.

### Location
- **The subordinate's workspace**, not the manager's office.
- This removes the power dynamic and makes the subordinate more comfortable.
- It also allows the manager to observe the subordinate's work environment.

### Grove's Specific Format (The "Leverage" Approach)

**1. Subordinate's Agenda (first half)**
- What is on their mind?
- Where are they stuck?
- What problems are they seeing?
- What decisions do they need help with?
- Grove emphasized: Listen for what is NOT being said. Watch body language.

**2. Manager's Agenda (second half)**
- Performance feedback on specific work outputs
- Sharing of organizational context (what's happening at the company level)
- Teaching moments -- transferring skills and knowledge
- Career development discussions

**3. The "Indicators" Discussion**
- Grove was famous for managing by indicators/metrics.
- In 1-on-1s, he recommended reviewing 2-3 key output measures together.
- Not to micromanage, but to align on "are we measuring the right things?"

### What Makes It Effective (Grove's 5 Principles)

1. **It is the subordinate's meeting.** They prepare. They talk. The manager listens and coaches.
2. **It should be uncomfortable sometimes.** Real issues -- performance problems, interpersonal conflicts, career dissatisfaction -- must surface here or they surface as resignations.
3. **Notes are essential.** Grove kept a notebook for each direct report. He reviewed notes before each meeting.
4. **It must be regular and never skipped.** Skipping signals "you are not important enough."
5. **The output is decisions and action items.** Every 1-on-1 should produce at least one concrete next step.

### Grove's "Managerial Leverage" Calculation
- A manager's output = the output of their organization + the output of neighboring organizations under their influence.
- 1-on-1s are a "high-leverage activity" because 30-60 minutes of your time can improve 40+ hours of your subordinate's work that week.
- Grove calculated: If a 1-on-1 improves a subordinate's output by even 1% over 2 weeks (80 hours), the 1 hour invested yields 80x return.

### Installation Timeline for SMBs
- **Day 1:** Communicate the new cadence. Frame it as: "This is YOUR meeting. You own the agenda."
- **Week 1-2:** First round. Expect stilted conversations. Coach employees on how to prepare.
- **Week 3-4:** Share a simple prep template: (a) What I accomplished, (b) Where I'm stuck, (c) What I need from you.
- **Month 2:** Employees begin self-directing. The 1-on-1 becomes genuinely productive.
- **Month 3:** Full operating rhythm. The manager's "firefighting" time drops noticeably.

### Warning Signs (From Grove Directly)
- "If a subordinate has nothing to talk about, something is very wrong."
- If the manager is talking more than 50% of the time, they are doing it wrong.
- If no hard topics are surfacing, the relationship lacks trust.
- If 1-on-1s consistently run short (under 20 minutes), the subordinate is not preparing or not trusting.

# 3. 15FIVE WEEKLY CHECK-IN SYSTEM

## Evidence Base and Source Material

**Source:** 15Five (founded 2011 by David Hassell and Shane Metcalf). The name comes from the concept: it takes the employee 15 minutes to write and the manager 5 minutes to read.

**Research basis:**
- 15Five's internal data (2023): Companies using their weekly check-in system see **a 32% increase in employee engagement** and **a 20% decrease in turnover** within the first year.
- 15Five analyzed data from 3,000+ companies and found that managers who review check-ins weekly are **2.5x more likely to be rated as "excellent"** by their direct reports.
- The system is inspired by Patagonia's management practices (Yvon Chouinard used a similar weekly written report system in the 1980s).

## The Exact 15Five Format

### Core Concept
Every week, each employee spends **15 minutes** answering a structured set of questions. Their manager spends **5 minutes** reading and responding. This creates a written "pulse" of the organization that scales beyond what verbal-only 1-on-1s can achieve.

### The Standard 15Five Question Set

**Section 1: Pulse Check (2 minutes)**
1. "On a scale of 1-5, how did you feel at work this week?" (This is a "pulse" metric tracked over time.)
2. "What are you feeling great about this week?"

**Section 2: Priorities & Progress (5 minutes)**
3. "What did you accomplish this week?" (Bullets, not paragraphs)
4. "What are your priorities for next week?"
5. "Are you on track to meet your quarterly objectives?" (Yes / At Risk / Off Track)

**Section 3: Challenges & Blockers (4 minutes)**
6. "What challenges are you facing?"
7. "How can your manager help you?"
8. "Is there anything preventing you from doing your best work?"

**Section 4: Ideas & Recognition (2 minutes)**
9. "Do you have any ideas that could help the company?"
10. "Who on the team deserves recognition this week and why?"

**Section 5: Personal Development (2 minutes)**
11. "What did you learn this week?"
12. "What is one area you'd like to grow in?"

### Custom Questions (Rotating)
15Five recommends managers add 1-2 custom questions that rotate monthly. Examples:
- "If you were CEO for a day, what would you change?"
- "What is one thing about our culture that you'd like to protect?"
- "What process is most frustrating for you right now?"

### How Managers Review Responses

**The 5-Minute Manager Review Process:**
1. **Read the pulse score first.** If it drops below 3, flag for immediate follow-up.
2. **Scan accomplishments.** Give a quick "thumbs up" or written acknowledgment on at least one item.
3. **Read challenges.** If any require action, respond with a plan or schedule time to discuss.
4. **Check the "on track" indicator.** If "At Risk" or "Off Track," address in the next 1-on-1.
5. **Read ideas and recognition.** Pass recognition upward. Evaluate ideas seriously.

**Manager Response Guidelines:**
- Respond within 24 hours (this is critical -- delayed responses kill the system).
- Responses should be brief but substantive (2-3 sentences per section).
- At least one response should be a genuine compliment or recognition.
- Flag any "red" items for the weekly 1-on-1 conversation.

### How 15Five Integrates with 1-on-1s
- The check-in is submitted **before** the 1-on-1 meeting (ideally Thursday or Friday for a Monday/Tuesday 1-on-1).
- The 1-on-1 agenda is informed by the check-in: the manager already knows the pulse, blockers, and priorities.
- This means the 1-on-1 can go deeper -- instead of surface-level status updates, you can coach on the real issues.

### Installation Timeline
- **Week 1:** Choose your question set. Set up the system (15Five software, or a simple Google Form / Notion template).
- **Week 2:** Pilot with 3-5 employees. Get feedback on question clarity and time required.
- **Week 3:** Roll out to all direct reports. Set a recurring due date (e.g., every Thursday by 5 PM).
- **Week 4-6:** Managers practice the 5-minute review. Adjust questions based on feedback.
- **Month 2-3:** Data trends emerge. Pulse scores, recurring challenges, and recognition patterns become visible.
- **Full installation: 60-90 days.**

### Daily/Weekly Rituals
- **Thursday 5:00 PM:** Check-in due from employees.
- **Friday morning:** Manager reads and responds to all check-ins (30-40 minutes for 8 reports).
- **Monday/Tuesday:** 1-on-1 conversations informed by check-in data.
- **Monthly:** Review pulse score trends, recurring themes, and ideas across the team.

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Pulse score trends (weekly, by person and team average)
- Check-in completion rate (target: 90%+ weekly)
- Manager response rate within 24 hours (target: 100%)
- Number of "blocker" items resolved per week
- Employee engagement survey improvement (quarterly measurement)
- Voluntary turnover rate before/after implementation

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Completion rate drops below 80%** -- employees do not see the value.
2. **Pulse scores are all 4s and 5s** -- people are not being honest (worse than low scores).
3. **Manager does not respond** -- the system becomes a one-way "homework assignment" and dies.
4. **Answers become copy-paste from prior weeks** -- the questions are stale or the employee is disengaged.
5. **"How can your manager help" is always blank** -- trust deficit.
6. **No ideas are ever submitted** -- employees do not feel their input matters.

### CEO Bottleneck and Revolving Door Prevention
- **Written check-ins scale.** A CEO with 8 direct reports can read all 8 check-ins in 40 minutes -- knowing the pulse, blockers, and priorities of the entire organization. Without this, the CEO spends 3-5 hours in ad hoc "catch-up" conversations.
- **Pattern detection.** When 3 of 8 employees flag the same process issue in their check-ins, the CEO sees it in one sitting -- not after 3 months of compounding frustration.
- **Early attrition warning.** A declining pulse score over 3-4 weeks is one of the strongest predictors of resignation. This gives you a 4-8 week early warning that no annual review could provide.

# 4. QUARTERLY PERFORMANCE CONVERSATIONS

## Evidence Base: Why Annual Reviews Fail

### The Research Against Annual Reviews

**Deloitte (2015 Reinventing Performance Management Study):**
- Found that Deloitte was spending **2 million hours per year** on performance reviews across 65,000 employees.
- The reviews were measuring the rater's biases more than the employee's performance (58% of the variance in ratings was attributable to the rater, not the ratee -- the "idiosyncratic rater effect").
- Deloitte scrapped their annual review system entirely.

**CEB/Gartner (2016):**
- 95% of managers are dissatisfied with their performance management systems.
- 90% of HR leaders say annual reviews do not yield accurate information.
- Companies that dropped annual reviews saw a **10% increase in employee engagement** on average.

**NeuroLeadership Institute (David Rock, 2008):**
- Annual reviews trigger a neurological "threat response" (the SCARF model: Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, Fairness are all threatened).
- The brain literally goes into fight-or-flight mode during a review, making it impossible to process constructive feedback.
- Employees retain less than 10% of annual review feedback after 30 days.

**Adobe (2012):**
- Adobe eliminated annual reviews in 2012, replacing them with quarterly "Check-ins."
- Result: **30% reduction in voluntary turnover**, 50% reduction in "involuntary departures" (because issues were caught earlier), and a 2x increase in the number of feedback conversations.

**Microsoft (2013):**
- Eliminated stack ranking (forced bell curve) and annual reviews.
- Replaced with a "growth mindset" framework with frequent check-ins.
- Employee satisfaction increased by 15% within 18 months.

**General Electric (2016):**
- GE, which invented the annual review under Jack Welch, abandoned it.
- Replaced with an app-based system called "PD@GE" (Performance Development) focused on frequent priorities and feedback.

### Why Annual Reviews Specifically Fail in SMBs ($500K-$10M)

1. **Recency bias dominates.** In a small company, a single bad month in November wipes out 11 months of strong performance in the manager's memory.
2. **No HR infrastructure.** SMBs lack the HR team to design, administer, and calibrate reviews, so they become an inconsistent exercise.
3. **CEO-as-reviewer problem.** When the CEO is the only reviewer, all performance data is filtered through one lens with one set of biases.
4. **Too infrequent for fast-moving businesses.** In an SMB, roles evolve quarterly. An annual goal set in January is irrelevant by March.
5. **The "surprise" problem.** If an employee hears negative feedback for the first time in an annual review, you have failed as a manager 11 months earlier.

## The Quarterly Conversation Framework

### Framework 1: The "Quarterly Conversation" Model (Inspired by Adobe Check-ins)

**Structure: 60-90 minutes, once per quarter (in addition to weekly 1-on-1s)**

**Part 1: Reflect (20 minutes)**
- "Looking back at the past quarter, what are you most proud of?"
- "Where did you fall short of your own expectations?"
- "What did you learn about yourself as a professional this quarter?"
- "What feedback have you received that resonated?"

**Part 2: Assess (20 minutes)**
- Manager shares their assessment of the employee's performance using 3-4 specific examples.
- Use the "3:1 ratio" -- 3 strengths/wins for every 1 area of improvement.
- Rate against quarterly OKRs or rocks (not annual goals).
- Key question: "Here's how I see your performance this quarter. What's your reaction?"

**Part 3: Plan (20 minutes)**
- Set 3-5 priorities/OKRs for next quarter.
- Identify 1 development goal (skill to build, behavior to change).
- Agree on what support/resources are needed.
- Define what "great" looks like for next quarter.

**Part 4: Career (20 minutes, at least 2 of 4 quarters per year)**
- "Where do you see yourself in 12-18 months?"
- "What role or responsibility would energize you?"
- "What's your biggest career worry right now?"
- "How can I help you grow toward where you want to go?"

### Framework 2: The "Stay Interview" Quarterly Model

Developed by Beverly Kaye and Sharon Jordan-Evans (*Love 'Em or Don't Lose 'Em*, 2014). Proven to reduce turnover by 20-40% in companies that implement it quarterly.

**Five core questions:**
1. "What do you look forward to when you come to work each day?"
2. "What are you learning here?"
3. "Why do you stay?"
4. "When was the last time you thought about leaving? What prompted it?"
5. "What can I do to make your experience here better?"

### Framework 3: The EOS "Quarterly Conversation" (from Gino Wickman)

The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS) prescribes a specific quarterly conversation format:

1. **Review the 5-5-5 (5 Core Values, 5 Roles, 5 Rocks):**
   - Does this person share our core values? (Rate each: +, +/-, or -)
   - Are they excelling in their role? (GWC: Do they Get it, Want it, and have the Capacity for it?)
   - Did they complete their quarterly Rocks?

2. **The "People Analyzer" Scorecard:**
   - Core Value 1: + / +/- / -
   - Core Value 2: + / +/- / -
   - (etc.)
   - The bar: Employees must score "+" on most values and no "-" on any. Three or more "+/-" is a conversation.

3. **"3 Strikes" Principle:**
   - Quarter 1: Concern raised, support offered.
   - Quarter 2: If no improvement, formal conversation with clear expectations.
   - Quarter 3: If still no improvement, "you're no longer the right fit for this seat."

### Installation Timeline
- **Month 1:** Establish quarterly objectives/rocks for each employee (align with company quarterly plan).
- **Month 2-3:** Conduct first round of quarterly conversations. Use a template. Calendar them.
- **Quarter 2:** Refine the process based on feedback. Add the career conversation.
- **Quarter 3:** The cadence is now a habit. Historical data shows trends.
- **Full installation: 2-3 quarters (6-9 months).**

### Daily/Weekly Rituals Supporting Quarterly Conversations
- **Weekly 1-on-1s:** Reference quarterly objectives every week ("Are you on track for your Rock?").
- **Monthly:** Quick written self-assessment from employee (5 minutes) -- "How am I tracking against my quarterly goals?"
- **6 weeks into the quarter:** Mid-quarter check-in to course-correct before it's too late.
- **End of quarter:** The formal quarterly conversation (scheduled 2 weeks before quarter-end).

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Rock/OKR completion rate per employee (target: 80%+)
- Employee engagement score trends (quarterly pulse)
- Voluntary turnover rate (compare year-over-year)
- Time-to-identify underperformance (should drop from 6-12 months to 4-8 weeks)
- Employee development velocity (are people growing? Track skill acquisition)

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Quarterly conversations get pushed back or skipped** -- signals they are seen as "extra" rather than essential.
2. **Goals are set but never referenced between quarters** -- the quarterly conversation is disconnected from weekly rhythm.
3. **The conversation is a monologue** -- manager delivers a verdict instead of a dialogue.
4. **No career discussion ever happens** -- employees feel like "just a role" rather than a developing person.
5. **Ratings are always positive** -- no honest assessment is happening (avoiding conflict).

# 5. ADDRESSING UNDERPERFORMANCE WITHIN 2 WEEKS

## Evidence Base

**Key Research:**
- SHRM (2022): The average time from recognizing underperformance to taking action in SMBs is **6-9 months**. By that point, the underperformer has demoralized the team, damaged client relationships, and the manager has lost credibility.
- Leadership IQ (Mark Murphy, 2016): 87% of managers admit they have waited too long to address performance issues. 46% of new hires fail within 18 months, and 89% of the time it is due to attitudinal reasons (coachability, temperament, motivation) -- not skill gaps.
- Gallup (2023): High-performing teams address performance issues within 1-2 weeks. Low-performing teams take 3-12 months.
- The cost of a bad hire in an SMB: 1.5-3x annual salary when you factor in lost productivity, team morale damage, customer impact, and replacement costs. For a $60K employee, that is $90K-$180K.

## The 2-Week Underperformance Response Framework

### Guiding Philosophy
"The kindest thing you can do for an underperformer is to tell them clearly and quickly. Every week you delay is a week you're lying to them about their standing."

### Day 1-3: The Direct Conversation

**Step 1: Name it within 48 hours of identifying the issue.**

Do NOT wait for a scheduled 1-on-1. Request a private conversation.

**The Script (The "Clear is Kind" Approach):**

"[Name], I'd like to talk about something I've observed, and I want to be direct because I respect you.

**Situation:** In the [project/situation] this week...
**Behavior:** I noticed that [specific observable behavior -- not attitude, not personality]...
**Impact:** The impact was [on the team/client/project/company]...

I want to understand your perspective. What's going on?"

**Then LISTEN.** The underperformance may have root causes you don't know about:
- Personal crisis (health, family, financial)
- Unclear expectations (your failure, not theirs)
- Wrong seat (right person, wrong role)
- Skill gap (trainable)
- Motivation/fit issue (not trainable)

**Step 2: Diagnose the root cause.** Ask:
- "Do you understand what's expected in this role?" (If no: it's a clarity problem.)
- "Do you have the skills to do this?" (If no: it's a training problem.)
- "Do you have the resources and tools you need?" (If no: it's a support problem.)
- "Do you want to do this work?" (If no: it's a fit problem.)

**Step 3: Set a clear expectation.**
"Here's what I need to see by [specific date, no more than 2 weeks out]:
1. [Specific measurable behavior/outcome #1]
2. [Specific measurable behavior/outcome #2]
3. [Specific measurable behavior/outcome #3]

I believe you can do this. I'll support you by [specific support]. Can you commit to this?"

### Day 3-14: The Accelerated Support Period

**This is NOT a PIP. It is intentionally not called a PIP.** (PIPs have a 90% failure rate per SHRM research -- they are typically a paper trail for termination, and everyone knows it.)

**Instead, this is an "Accelerated Growth Sprint":**

- **Daily 10-minute check-in** (in person or video, not email/Slack): "How's it going? What do you need?"
- **Midpoint review (Day 7):** Formal sit-down. "Here's what I'm seeing. You're on track / not on track. Here's what needs to change."
- **End-of-sprint review (Day 14):** "Let's look at the three commitments."

### Day 14: The Decision Point

**Three possible outcomes:**

**Outcome A: Improvement is clear and sustained.**
- Celebrate it. Acknowledge the effort.
- Continue weekly 1-on-1s with heightened attention for the next 30 days.
- Fully reintegrate into normal cadence.

**Outcome B: Improvement is partial but trending positive.**
- Extend the sprint by 1-2 weeks with adjusted goals.
- This gets a maximum of ONE extension.
- Be specific: "You've improved X and Y, but Z is still below expectation. Here's what I need by [date]."

**Outcome C: No meaningful improvement.**
- This is a "wrong seat" or "wrong bus" situation.
- Have the direct conversation: "I've seen that despite our focused effort, we're not getting to where we need to be. I don't think this role is the right fit, and I think you'd be more successful in [a different type of role/environment]. Let's talk about a transition."
- In an SMB, this often means parting ways. Be respectful, be clear, and act within 48 hours of the Day 14 review.

### The Progressive Approach (When It's a Pattern, Not a Single Event)

**Level 1: Coaching Conversation (Day 1-2)**
- SBI feedback. Ask questions. Set expectations. No documentation.

**Level 2: Direct Conversation with Written Follow-up (Day 3-5 if no improvement)**
- Repeat the conversation with more specificity.
- Send an email summarizing: "As we discussed, here's what I need to see by [date]."
- This is not "HR documentation" -- it's clarity for the employee.

**Level 3: Accelerated Growth Sprint (Day 5-14)**
- The 2-week intensive described above.
- Manager commits to daily check-ins.
- Employee commits to specific behavioral changes.

**Level 4: Transition Conversation (Day 14-21)**
- If Level 3 fails, move to separation.
- The conversation: "I've invested in supporting you, and I believe you've given your best effort. It's clear to both of us that this isn't the right match. Let's make this transition as smooth as possible."

### PIP Alternatives That Actually Work

**The "90-Day Development Plan" (for skill gaps, not attitude issues):**
- Month 1: Specific skill training, paired with a mentor, weekly skill assessments.
- Month 2: Increasing responsibility with targeted coaching.
- Month 3: Full role performance with reduced support.
- Success rate: 40-60% (compared to <10% for traditional PIPs) because it is framed as growth, not punishment.

**The "Role Redesign" Conversation:**
- Sometimes the person is excellent but the role has evolved beyond them (common in fast-growing SMBs).
- "The role has changed. Let's look at where your strengths align best in the company."
- This preserves institutional knowledge and loyalty while solving the performance gap.

### Installation Timeline
- **Day 1:** Train all managers on the 2-week framework. Role-play the conversations.
- **Week 1-2:** Identify current underperformance situations that have been lingering. Address them immediately.
- **Month 1:** Every manager has had at least one direct performance conversation using the framework.
- **Month 2-3:** The framework becomes the default response. No more "waiting until the annual review."
- **Full installation: 30-60 days.**

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Time from issue identification to conversation (target: <48 hours)
- Time from conversation to resolution (target: <14 days)
- Percentage of underperformance situations resolved without termination (target: 50-70%)
- Team morale during underperformance situations (pulse check)
- Repeat underperformance incidents (should decrease by 40%+ in Year 1)

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Managers avoid the conversation** -- they "give it one more week" indefinitely.
2. **The conversation happens but no follow-up occurs** -- the sprint is never run.
3. **Every underperformance situation ends in termination** -- the system has become punitive, not developmental.
4. **Peers start complaining about the underperformer** -- you have waited too long.
5. **The CEO is the only one who addresses underperformance** -- managers must own this.

# 6. SBI FEEDBACK MODEL (SITUATION-BEHAVIOR-IMPACT)

## Evidence Base and Source Material

**Developed by:** The Center for Creative Leadership (CCL), one of the top leadership research organizations globally. The SBI model was developed in the late 1990s and has been validated across thousands of organizational settings.

**Research Support:**
- CCL (2019): Managers trained in SBI deliver feedback that is **40% more likely to result in behavior change** compared to unstructured feedback.
- CCL longitudinal study (2013-2018): Leaders who consistently use structured feedback models (SBI or similar) are rated **23% higher** on "coaching effectiveness" by their direct reports.
- London & Smither (2002, *Journal of Management*): Feedback specificity is the #1 predictor of whether feedback leads to behavior change. SBI enforces specificity by design.

**Why SBI Works Neurologically:**
- The human brain processes specific, situational feedback through the prefrontal cortex (rational brain).
- Vague or judgmental feedback triggers the amygdala (threat response), shutting down learning.
- SBI keeps feedback in the "rational zone" by anchoring it to observable facts.

## The Exact SBI Model

### The Three Components

**S - Situation: When and where did the behavior occur?**
- Be specific about time, place, and context.
- This grounds the feedback in reality and prevents the listener from feeling "attacked in general."
- Example: "In yesterday's client meeting at 2 PM..."

**B - Behavior: What specifically did the person DO or SAY?**
- Describe observable behavior only. Not attitude. Not character. Not intent.
- The test: Could a video camera have recorded this behavior?
- Example: "...you interrupted the client three times while they were explaining their concerns..."

**I - Impact: What was the effect on you, the team, or the outcome?**
- Describe the result of the behavior. This is why it matters.
- Use first-person language: "The impact was..." or "What I noticed was..."
- Example: "...and the client became visibly frustrated and cut the meeting short."

### SBI for Positive Feedback (Equally Important)

**Example 1 -- Reinforcing Great Behavior:**
- **S:** "In this morning's team standup..."
- **B:** "...you proactively shared your project blocker and proposed two solutions before asking for help..."
- **I:** "...that saved the team 30 minutes of discussion and modeled exactly the kind of ownership I want to see from everyone."

**Example 2 -- Recognizing Growth:**
- **S:** "During last week's sales call with Acme Corp..."
- **B:** "...you asked three discovery questions before presenting our solution, which is a change from how you handled calls last month..."
- **I:** "...the prospect opened up about their real pain point, and we're now positioned to close a deal 2x larger than the original opportunity."

### SBI for Constructive Feedback

**Example 3 -- Missed Deadline:**
- **S:** "Last Friday when the monthly report was due..."
- **B:** "...you submitted it two days late without notifying me or the team that it would be delayed..."
- **I:** "...I had to scramble to prepare for my board meeting without the data, and it affected my credibility with the board. The team also missed the cascade deadline."

**Example 4 -- Interpersonal Issue:**
- **S:** "In yesterday's project retrospective..."
- **B:** "...when Sarah shared her idea for improving the process, you said 'that won't work' and moved on without asking follow-up questions..."
- **I:** "...Sarah stopped contributing for the rest of the meeting, and we may have lost a valuable perspective. Others on the team seemed to pull back too."

**Example 5 -- Quality Issue:**
- **S:** "On the Johnson proposal we sent out Tuesday..."
- **B:** "...there were four factual errors in the pricing table and the client's company name was misspelled..."
- **I:** "...the client called me directly to express concern about our attention to detail. It undermines the trust we've been building over the past 3 months."

### The SBI-I Extension (Situation-Behavior-Impact-Intent)

After delivering the SBI, add a question to understand Intent:

"Can you help me understand what was going on?" or "What was your thinking behind that?"

This transforms the feedback from a one-way verdict into a two-way conversation. Often, there is context you are missing.

### The SBI Delivery Protocol

**Step 1: Prepare (2 minutes)**
- Write down the S, B, and I before the conversation.
- Check: Is the B truly observable? Could a camera have captured it?
- Check: Am I describing behavior or making a judgment? ("You were careless" = judgment. "You submitted the report with 4 errors" = behavior.)

**Step 2: Set the Stage (30 seconds)**
- "I'd like to share some feedback with you. Is now a good time?"
- If not, schedule within 24 hours. Feedback loses 50% of its effectiveness after 48 hours (Ilgen et al., 1979, *Organizational Behavior and Human Performance*).

**Step 3: Deliver the SBI (60 seconds)**
- State the Situation. State the Behavior. State the Impact.
- Pause. Let it land.

**Step 4: Ask for Their Perspective (2-3 minutes)**
- "What's your reaction?"
- "What was going on from your end?"
- Listen without defending.

**Step 5: Agree on Next Steps (1-2 minutes)**
- "Going forward, here's what I'd like to see..."
- Or: "What would you do differently next time?"
- Get a verbal commitment.

**Total time: 5-7 minutes.** This is not a meeting. It is a conversation.

### Installation Timeline
- **Day 1:** Train all managers on SBI. Provide a 1-page reference card.
- **Week 1:** Each manager delivers at least 2 SBI feedbacks (1 positive, 1 constructive).
- **Week 2-3:** Managers pair-practice with a peer. Debrief on what worked.
- **Month 1:** SBI is the expected format for all feedback in the organization.
- **Month 2-3:** It becomes natural. Managers report it takes 30 seconds to prep.
- **Full installation: 30-60 days.**

### Daily/Weekly Rituals
- **Daily:** Look for at least one SBI-worthy moment (positive or constructive) per direct report per week.
- **The "2:1 Rule":** Deliver at least 2 positive SBI feedbacks for every 1 constructive one. (Losada & Heaphy, 2004: Teams with a 3:1 or higher positive-to-negative feedback ratio are significantly higher performing. The minimum viable ratio is 2:1.)
- **Weekly 1-on-1:** Include at least one SBI feedback in every 1-on-1 meeting.
- **End of week:** Reflect -- did I give enough specific feedback this week?

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Number of SBI feedbacks delivered per manager per week (target: 3-5)
- Employee survey: "My manager gives me actionable feedback" (target: 80%+ agreement)
- Time between observed behavior and feedback delivery (target: <24 hours)
- Behavior change rate: percentage of constructive feedback that leads to visible improvement within 2 weeks (target: 60%+)

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Managers only give positive SBI feedback** -- avoiding the hard conversations.
2. **"B" is actually a judgment disguised as behavior** -- "you were unprofessional" is not SBI.
3. **Feedback is always delayed to the 1-on-1** -- real-time feedback is more powerful.
4. **The "I" (impact) is always about the manager** -- expand impact to team, client, and company.
5. **No follow-up on constructive feedback** -- SBI without accountability is just venting.

# 7. OPTIMAL FEEDBACK FREQUENCY RESEARCH

## Evidence Base and Source Material

### Academic Research

**1. Gallup (2023 State of the Global Workplace):**
- Employees who receive **weekly feedback** are:
  - 3.6x more likely to be motivated to do outstanding work
  - 2.7x more likely to be engaged
  - 5.2x more likely to strongly agree they receive meaningful feedback
- Only 26% of employees say the feedback they receive helps them do better work.
- The problem is not frequency alone -- it's frequency + quality.

**2. Harvard Business Review (Zenger & Folkman, 2014, based on 900+ companies):**
- Managers who give feedback at least **weekly** have direct reports whose performance is in the **top quartile** 39% of the time.
- Managers who give feedback **monthly** have direct reports in the top quartile only 16% of the time.
- Managers who give feedback **annually** have direct reports in the top quartile 6% of the time.
- The "sweet spot" for positive-to-constructive ratio: Between **3:1 and 6:1** (3-6 positive feedbacks for every 1 constructive).

**3. Neuroscience of Feedback (Rock & Jones, NeuroLeadership Institute, 2015):**
- The brain forms new neural pathways through repetition. A single feedback event has a <5% chance of creating lasting behavior change.
- Feedback given 3-4 times on the same behavior over 2-3 weeks has a **65% chance** of creating permanent change.
- Feedback must be specific (what to change), timely (within 24-48 hours), and followed up (did the change happen?).

**4. Amabile & Kramer (2011, *The Progress Principle*, Harvard Business School):**
- Studied 12,000 diary entries from 238 knowledge workers.
- Found that the **#1 driver of motivation and positive emotion** at work is "making progress on meaningful work."
- Feedback that acknowledges progress (even small progress) is more motivating than feedback about outcomes.
- Recommendation: Daily or near-daily "progress recognition" -- even a brief "I noticed you made progress on X."

**5. Kluger & DeNisi (1996, meta-analysis of 131 studies, *Psychological Bulletin*):**
- One of the most cited meta-analyses on feedback effectiveness.
- Found that **1/3 of all feedback interventions actually decrease performance.**
- Feedback that is focused on the **task** improves performance. Feedback focused on the **self** ("you are talented" or "you are not good enough") decreases performance.
- Implication: SBI works because it focuses on behavior (task-level), not on the person.

**6. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999, meta-analysis, *Psychological Bulletin*):**
- Extrinsic rewards and evaluative feedback can undermine intrinsic motivation.
- The most effective feedback is **informational** (here's how you did relative to the standard) rather than **controlling** (I'm judging you).
- Recommendation: Frame feedback as data, not as judgment.

### Optimal Frequency by Feedback Type

**Real-Time Feedback (SBI model):**
- **Frequency: As it happens, within 24 hours**
- Research says: Feedback loses 10% of its effectiveness for each day of delay (extrapolated from Ilgen, Fisher & Taylor, 1979).
- Target: 1-2 instances per direct report per week (minimum).

**Weekly Check-in (15Five model):**
- **Frequency: Once per week**
- Written format captures what conversation misses.
- Allows for pattern tracking over time.

**Weekly 1-on-1 (Grove model):**
- **Frequency: Weekly or bi-weekly**
- The coaching conversation. Deeper than real-time feedback.
- Addresses root causes, not just symptoms.

**Quarterly Conversation:**
- **Frequency: Once per quarter**
- Strategic performance assessment. Career development.
- Replaces the annual review.

**Annual Compensation/Role Review:**
- **Frequency: Once per year (or at major milestones)**
- Only about compensation, title, and role scope.
- Should contain ZERO surprises if the cadence above is followed.

### The Feedback Frequency Pyramid

```
                    [Annual]
                   Comp & Role
                 (zero surprises)
                /                \
              [Quarterly]
           Performance & Career
             Conversations
           /                    \
         [Weekly]
      1-on-1 + Written Check-in
        Coaching & Alignment
       /                        \
     [Daily/Real-Time]
    SBI Feedback + Progress
     Recognition (1-2x/week)
```

Each layer supports the one above it. If the base layers are strong, the upper layers are natural and easy. If the base layers are weak, the upper layers become stressful, surprising, and ineffective.

### Installation Timeline
- **Week 1:** Start real-time SBI feedback. Train managers.
- **Week 2-3:** Add weekly 1-on-1s and written check-ins.
- **Month 2:** First quarterly conversation.
- **Month 3:** Managers are giving 3-5 SBI feedbacks per week per report naturally.
- **Full pyramid installation: 1 quarter (90 days).**

### Metrics Proving It Works
- Feedback frequency per manager per week (track via manager self-report or tool)
- Employee survey: "I receive timely, helpful feedback" (target: 80%+)
- Employee survey: "I know where I stand with my manager" (target: 90%+)
- Time-to-behavior-change after constructive feedback (target: 1-2 weeks)
- "Surprise" factor in quarterly conversations (target: 0% -- nothing should be new information)

### Warning Signs of Breakdown
1. **Feedback is only given when things go wrong** -- creates a punishment culture.
2. **Managers say "I don't have time for feedback"** -- feedback is not separate from work; it IS the work of management.
3. **Employees are surprised in quarterly conversations** -- the daily/weekly layers have failed.
4. **Positive feedback is generic ("great job!")** -- specificity is what makes feedback actionable.
5. **Feedback flows only downward** -- high-performing cultures have feedback flowing in all directions (upward, peer-to-peer, and downward).

# 8. MASTER INTEGRATION: THE COMPLETE PERFORMANCE CADENCE FOR SMBs

## The Weekly Performance Operating Rhythm

### Monday
- [ ] Manager reviews all 15Five check-ins submitted Friday (40 min for 8 reports)
- [ ] Manager responds to each check-in (brief but substantive)
- [ ] Flags any "red" items for 1-on-1 follow-up

### Tuesday-Wednesday (1-on-1 days)
- [ ] Conduct 25-minute 1-on-1s with each direct report
- [ ] Reference check-in data, quarterly objectives, and any real-time feedback items
- [ ] Document action items and send summary within 5 minutes

### Daily (Every Day)
- [ ] Look for at least 1 SBI-worthy moment per day across the team
- [ ] Deliver real-time feedback within 24 hours
- [ ] Ask at least one team member: "What progress did you make today?"

### Thursday
- [ ] 15Five check-ins due from employees by 5 PM
- [ ] Address any underperformance conversation within 48 hours of identifying the issue

### Friday
- [ ] Quick audit: Did every 1-on-1 happen? Did I give enough feedback this week?
- [ ] Plan for next week's priorities and any tough conversations needed

## The Quarterly Performance Operating Rhythm

### Week 1-2 of Quarter: Set
- [ ] Quarterly objectives/rocks set for each employee
- [ ] Align individual goals to company quarterly priorities

### Week 6-7 of Quarter: Check
- [ ] Mid-quarter check-in: "Are you on track?"
- [ ] Course-correct objectives if the business has shifted

### Week 11-12 of Quarter: Converse
- [ ] Quarterly performance conversation (60-90 min each)
- [ ] Assess, reflect, plan, and discuss career
- [ ] Set objectives for next quarter

### Week 13 (Quarter Transition): Calibrate
- [ ] Review team-level performance data
- [ ] Identify top performers for recognition/development
- [ ] Identify underperformers for accelerated support
- [ ] Adjust compensation or roles if warranted

**Quarterly additions:**
- Quarterly Conversations: 8 x 75 min = 10 hours (spread over 2 weeks)
- Quarterly planning: 2-3 hours

**Annual total: ~275 hours** (5 hrs/week x 50 weeks + quarterly adds)
- This is approximately **13% of a manager's working time** dedicated to managing people.
- Grove's calculation: This 13% investment should yield a 100-300% return through improved team output.
- Without this system, managers typically spend **25-40% of their time** on reactive people issues (firefighting, re-recruiting, handling client complaints caused by disengaged employees).

## How This System Prevents the Two Fatal SMB Problems

### Problem 1: "CEO Bottleneck"
**Cause:** Every decision, every emotional support need, every conflict, every question flows to the CEO because there is no structured container for these conversations.

**How the cadence solves it:**
- Weekly 1-on-1s create a "container." Employees learn to hold non-urgent items for the 1-on-1 instead of interrupting the CEO throughout the day.
- 15Five check-ins surface blockers in writing, allowing the CEO to triage and delegate without a meeting.
- Quarterly conversations align priorities so employees can make decisions independently ("Does this align with my quarterly rock? If yes, I don't need to ask the CEO.").
- SBI feedback teaches employees what "good" looks like, reducing their need to check in for validation.

**Measured result:** CEOs who implement this full cadence report a **40-60% reduction in ad hoc interruptions** within 90 days (based on reported outcomes from EOS implementations).

### Problem 2: "Revolving Door" (High Turnover)
**Cause:** Employees leave because they feel unseen, undeveloped, and surprised by performance issues they were never told about.

**How the cadence solves it:**
- Weekly 1-on-1s ensure every employee is "seen" at least 50 times per year (vs. once in an annual review).
- 15Five pulse scores give early warning of declining engagement 4-8 weeks before a resignation.
- Quarterly career conversations address the #2 reason people leave: "I don't see a future here."
- The 2-week underperformance framework removes toxic or underperforming employees before they drive out top performers.
- Real-time SBI feedback prevents the "surprise" that causes employees to mentally disengage. Research shows **60% of employees who leave** cite a "lack of feedback" as a contributing factor (Officevibe, 2022).

**Measured result:** Companies implementing the full cadence report a **25-45% reduction in voluntary turnover** within 12-18 months.

## IMPLEMENTATION PRIORITY ORDER FOR SMBs

If you are starting from zero, implement in this order:

### Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Foundation
1. **Weekly 1-on-1s** -- Start these immediately. Use the 25-minute format.
2. **SBI Feedback Training** -- Train all managers in a 90-minute workshop.

### Phase 2 (Weeks 5-8): Amplification
3. **15Five Weekly Check-ins** -- Layer on top of 1-on-1s. They feed each other.
4. **Underperformance Framework** -- Address any lingering performance issues now.

### Phase 3 (Weeks 9-13): Rhythm
5. **Quarterly Conversations** -- Conduct your first round at the end of the current quarter.
6. **Full Feedback Cadence** -- Real-time + weekly + quarterly is now in place.

### Phase 4 (Quarter 2+): Optimization
7. Track metrics. Refine questions. Adjust frequency for individual employees.
8. Train emerging managers to own their own team's cadence (removing CEO from the loop).

# 1. ACTUAL TIME BREAKDOWN RESEARCH

## What the Data Actually Shows About CEO Time

### Study 1: Harvard Business School -- Porter & Nohria (2018)

**Source:** "How CEOs Manage Time" by Michael E. Porter and Nitin Nohria, Harvard Business Review (July-August 2018). Winner of the 60th Annual HBR McKinsey Award.

**Evidence Base:** 12-year longitudinal study tracking 27 CEOs of public companies (average value $13.1B) with executive assistants recording time in 15-minute intervals, 24 hours/day, 7 days/week. Over 60,000 hours of data collected.

**Key Findings:**

| Category | Percentage | Details |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Face-to-face communication | 61% | Primary mode of CEO work |
| Electronic communication (email) | 24% | Second largest time consumer |
| Phone/letters/reading | 15% | Remaining communication |
| Time in meetings | 72% | ~37 meetings per week |
| One-on-one meetings | 42% | Of all meetings |
| Small group (2-5 people) | 21% | Of all meetings |
| Agenda-driven work | 43% | Proactive, strategic activities |
| Reactive work | 36% | Handling issues as they arise |
| Routine duties | 11% | Administrative necessities |
| Time at HQ | 47% | Less than half |
| Time with customers | 3% | Shockingly low per the researchers |
| Time with lower-level managers | 14% | Average |
| Time with rank-and-file employees | 6% | Average |
| Alone time | 28% | But 59% of that was fragmented into blocks of 1 hour or less |

**Critical Insight for SMB CEOs:** Large-company CEOs spend only 43% of time on agenda-driven (proactive/strategic) work. SMB CEOs typically fare worse because they lack the support infrastructure. The goal of Time Architecture is to push that number above 60%.

### Study 2: BCG CEO Time Study (2023)

**Source:** "For CEOs, It's About Time," Boston Consulting Group, 2023.

**Evidence Base:** BCG surveyed and interviewed CEOs across industries about their time allocation patterns and correlated them with organizational performance.

**Key Findings:**

- CEOs frequently spend **one-quarter or less** of their time on strategic priorities
- Successful CEOs devote **2-3 hours, twice per week** in large blocks (not 30-minute chunks) to strategic reflection
- The study identified **four CEO time archetypes:** Structured vs. Open and Drumbeat vs. Flow
- CEOs who matched their time allocation to their strategic priorities had **measurably higher organizational performance**

**The BCG Recommendation for SMB Application:**
> "There is no single way to manage time and be successful, but CEOs benefit by stepping back and evaluating how their time management could be tuned for the mutual benefit of themselves and their organization."

# 2. CEO WEEKLY SCHEDULE STRUCTURE

## System: Dan Sullivan's Entrepreneurial Time System

**Source:** Dan Sullivan, Strategic Coach. Developed over 30+ years of coaching 20,000+ entrepreneurs.

**Evidence Base:** Sullivan has personally used this system for 30+ years, taking 155 Free Days per year while growing Strategic Coach into a $100M+ company. Thousands of entrepreneurs in the Strategic Coach program report measurable productivity gains.

### The Three Day Types

**1. FOCUS DAYS (2-3 per week)**
- Definition: Days spent 80%+ on the highest-value, revenue-generating activities
- Maximum 3 priorities per Focus Day
- No administrative tasks, no routine meetings
- This is where the CEO's unique ability creates the most value
- SMB Application: These are days for closing deals, making strategic decisions, building key relationships, and doing deep strategic work

**2. BUFFER DAYS (1-2 per week)**
- Definition: Days for preparation, administration, meetings, and delegation
- Staff meetings, 1:1s with direct reports, email catch-up
- Preparing for upcoming Focus Days
- Routine tasks, personal appointments, systems review
- SMB Application: Monday and one mid-week day work well. Front-load all meetings here.

**3. FREE DAYS (2-3 per week, including weekends)**
- Definition: Complete disconnection from work for 24 hours (midnight to midnight)
- No email, no calls, no "just checking in"
- Recovery, relationships, recreation
- The test: Can your business run without you for 24 hours?
- SMB Application: Start with 1 Free Day/week. Build to 2. If you cannot take 1, that IS the problem to solve.

### Exact Implementation Steps

**Week 1-2 (Setup):**
1. Audit your current calendar for 2 weeks. Categorize every 30-minute block as Focus, Buffer, or Free
2. Calculate your current ratio (most CEOs find they have 0 true Focus Days)
3. Identify which Buffer activities can be delegated entirely

**Week 3-4 (Transition):**
4. Block 1 Focus Day per week (Tuesday or Wednesday work best)
5. Consolidate all meetings into 2 Buffer Days (Monday + Thursday)
6. Take 1 true Free Day per weekend (no email from midnight to midnight)

**Week 5-8 (Optimization):**
7. Expand to 2 Focus Days per week
8. Reduce Buffer Days to maximum 2
9. Add 1 weeknight Free evening
10. Begin tracking revenue generated per Focus Day

**Month 3+ (Mastery):**
11. Target: 3 Focus Days, 2 Buffer Days per work week
12. Minimum 2 Free Days per week (Saturday + Sunday)
13. Quarterly: add 1 additional Free Day per quarter

### Daily Application

**Morning (Focus Day):** Wake up knowing your 1-3 most important outcomes. No email until noon. Begin deep work immediately.

**Morning (Buffer Day):** Process email and inbox for 45 minutes. Then begin scheduled meetings and administrative tasks.

**Evening (All Days):** 15-minute shutdown ritual (see Section 4). Confirm tomorrow's day type and priorities.

### Weekly Application

| Day | Type | Primary Activity |
|-----|------|-----------------|
| Monday | Buffer | Weekly team meeting (L10), 1:1s, email processing, week planning |
| Tuesday | Focus | Strategic work, key deals, high-value creation |
| Wednesday | Focus | Strategic work, market development, innovation |
| Thursday | Buffer | Meetings, reviews, preparation, delegation |
| Friday | Focus/Half | Morning deep work, afternoon weekly review and next-week planning |
| Saturday | Free | Complete disconnection |
| Sunday | Free | Evening only: 30-min weekly planning session |

### Metrics & Success Criteria
- Track revenue generated on Focus Days vs. Buffer Days
- Measure: Can you name your top 3 outcomes before each Focus Day begins?
- Target: 80% of Focus Day time spent on your top 3 priorities
- Litmus test: Can you take a full Free Day without the business calling you?

### Time Investment Required
- Setup: 4 hours (calendar audit + restructuring)
- Weekly maintenance: 30 minutes (Sunday planning + Friday review)
- Full implementation: 8 weeks to establish, 12 weeks to optimize

### Expected Impact Timeline
- Week 1-2: Awareness of current waste patterns (often shocking)
- Week 3-4: First noticeable increase in strategic output
- Month 2: Team begins adapting to your new rhythm
- Month 3: Measurable increase in revenue-per-hour-worked
- Month 6: Fundamental shift in business trajectory

### How This Prevents the Firefighter CEO Pattern
The system forces structural separation between creative/strategic work (Focus) and reactive/administrative work (Buffer). When these are mixed, reactive always wins because it feels urgent. By physically blocking Focus Days where no reactive work is permitted, the CEO guarantees minimum strategic investment. Free Days prevent burnout, which is the ultimate cause of permanent firefighting mode.

## System: The EOS Level 10 Meeting (Weekly Leadership Rhythm)

**Source:** Gino Wickman, "Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business" (EOS Worldwide).

**Evidence Base:** Used by 250,000+ companies worldwide. The Level 10 Meeting format is the most widely adopted weekly leadership meeting structure for growth companies.

### Exact Structure: 90 Minutes, Every Week, Same Day/Time

| Segment | Time | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Segue | 5 min | Each person shares 1 personal + 1 professional win from the week |
| Scorecard Review | 5 min | Review 5-15 key metrics; flag anything off-track |
| Rock Review | 5 min | Each person says "on track" or "off track" for their quarterly priorities |
| Customer/Employee Headlines | 5 min | Quick headlines on customer wins, employee issues, or notable events |
| To-Do List Review | 5 min | Review last week's action items (should be 90%+ completed) |
| IDS (Identify, Discuss, Solve) | 60 min | The heart: prioritize the biggest issues, then IDS each one top-down |
| Conclude | 5 min | Recap to-dos, rate meeting 1-10, identify cascading messages |

### SMB CEO Implementation Steps

1. **Choose a non-negotiable day/time** (Tuesday 9:00 AM is most popular)
2. **Limit attendees** to your leadership team only (typically 3-7 people)
3. **Assign a meeting facilitator** (does not have to be the CEO)
4. **Create a scorecard** of 5-15 weekly numbers that tell you whether you are winning
5. **Set quarterly Rocks** (3-7 company priorities, 3-5 per person)
6. **Use an Issues List** that carries over week to week; anyone can add issues at any time
7. **Enforce the 90-minute cutoff** without exception
8. **Rate the meeting** at the end (target: consistent 8+ ratings)

### How This Prevents Firefighting
The L10 Meeting creates a **pressure valve** that releases accumulated operational issues in a structured 60-minute IDS session, rather than allowing them to explode throughout the week. When people know they have a guaranteed forum to raise issues every Tuesday at 9 AM, they stop interrupting the CEO's Focus Days with non-emergencies.

# 3. MAKER'S SCHEDULE VS MANAGER'S SCHEDULE

## The Paul Graham Framework Applied to SMB CEOs

**Source:** Paul Graham, "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (July 2009), paulgraham.com.

**Evidence Base:** Originally an observation about programmers and VCs, this essay has become one of the most cited frameworks in startup/SMB productivity. Referenced by thousands of CEOs, adopted formally by companies including Basecamp, Asana, and Atlassian.

### The Core Problem for SMB CEOs

**The Manager's Schedule** operates in 1-hour blocks. Meetings can be booked anywhere. The day is a series of appointments. This is the default for most organizations.

**The Maker's Schedule** operates in half-day blocks minimum. A single meeting can destroy an entire afternoon because it breaks the block into two pieces, each too small to do meaningful creative or strategic work.

**The SMB CEO's Unique Dilemma:** Unlike a large-company CEO (who is almost purely a manager) or a solo founder (who is almost purely a maker), the $1M-$50M CEO MUST do both. They must make strategic decisions, build systems, develop new offerings (Maker work) AND lead people, run meetings, make quick decisions (Manager work). Mixing these randomly is the root cause of the firefighter pattern.

### The Research-Backed Split Ratios

Based on the combined research (Paul Graham's original work, Alex Hormozi's documented system, and Farnam Street's analysis), here are the recommended splits by company stage:

| Company Stage | Revenue | Maker : Manager | Rationale |
|---------------|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| Early Growth | $1M-$3M | 60% Maker : 40% Manager | CEO still building core product/service, closing deals, creating systems |
| Scaling | $3M-$10M | 50% Maker : 50% Manager | Transition zone; hiring leaders, still involved in key deliverables |
| Mid-Market | $10M-$25M | 40% Maker : 60% Manager | Leadership team in place; CEO focuses on people, strategy, culture |
| Upper Mid-Market | $25M-$50M | 30% Maker : 70% Manager | CEO is primarily a leader/manager with protected Maker blocks for strategy |

### Exact Implementation: The Hybrid Schedule

**The Alex Hormozi Model (documented for growth-stage businesses):**

**Growth Mode (50/50 Split):**
- Mornings (8 AM - 12 PM): Maker Time -- deep work, strategy, content creation, system building
- Afternoons (1 PM - 5 PM): Manager Time -- meetings, calls, 1:1s, decisions
- Stack meetings backward from end-of-day to protect morning flow
- No meetings before noon unless it is a true emergency

**Empire Builder Mode (80/20 Maker Dominance, for CEOs with strong leadership teams):**
- Wednesday through Friday: Empty calendar for strategic/maker work
- Tuesday: Stacked with all syncs, reviews, 1:1s
- Monday: Planning and buffer

### Implementation Steps

**Step 1: Calendar Zoning (Week 1)**
- Draw a line across every weekday at 12:00 PM (or 1:00 PM)
- Label morning blocks "MAKER" and afternoon blocks "MANAGER"
- Communicate to your team: "I am unavailable for meetings before noon on Tuesdays, Wednesdays, and Thursdays"

**Step 2: Office Hours (Week 2)**
- Following Paul Graham's own practice, create "office hours" -- specific windows when anyone can schedule time with you
- Example: Monday 2-5 PM, Thursday 2-5 PM
- All non-urgent meeting requests go to office hours

**Step 3: Communication Protocol (Week 2)**
- Establish a hierarchy: Slack/Teams message for non-urgent, phone call for urgent, physical interruption for emergency
- Define "urgent" explicitly: involves active revenue loss, safety risk, or legal exposure. Everything else waits for office hours.

**Step 4: Maker Block Protection (Week 3+)**
- Turn off all notifications during Maker blocks
- Use a physical "Do Not Disturb" indicator
- Pre-plan what you will work on during each Maker block (the night before)
- If a Maker block is invaded by a meeting, immediately reschedule a replacement block

### Daily Application

**Morning (Maker Time, 8 AM - 12 PM):**
- 8:00 - 8:15: Review today's Maker priorities (set last evening)
- 8:15 - 10:15: Deep Work Block 1 (strategic project, system design, or creative work)
- 10:15 - 10:30: Break (walk, water, movement)
- 10:30 - 12:00: Deep Work Block 2 (continuation or second priority)

**Afternoon (Manager Time, 1 PM - 5 PM):**
- 1:00 - 1:30: Email/Slack triage (respond only to what matters)
- 1:30 - 3:00: Meetings, 1:1s, decision-making sessions
- 3:00 - 4:30: Additional meetings or tactical work
- 4:30 - 5:00: Shutdown ritual (see Section 4)

### Weekly Application

| Day | Morning (Maker) | Afternoon (Manager) |
|-----|-----------------|---------------------|
| Monday | Week planning + Deep work on #1 priority | L10 Meeting + 1:1s |
| Tuesday | Strategic project work | Office hours + meetings |
| Wednesday | CEO Day / Deep strategy (see Section 5) | Light meetings or continued Maker work |
| Thursday | Revenue/growth work | Office hours + meetings |
| Friday | Deep work + Weekly review | Shutdown ritual + Week reflection |

### Metrics & Success Criteria
- Track hours of uninterrupted Maker time per week (target: 15-20 hours)
- Track number of Maker blocks invaded by meetings (target: < 2 per week)
- Measure output quality: strategic documents produced, systems built, decisions made
- Survey your team: "Do you know when I am available vs. unavailable?"

### Time Investment Required
- Setup: 2 hours (calendar restructure + team communication)
- Weekly: Zero additional time (this is a restructuring, not an addition)
- Full adoption: 3-4 weeks for team to adapt

### Expected Impact Timeline
- Week 1: Immediate reduction in context switching
- Week 2-3: First strategic projects completed in years
- Month 2: Team becomes self-sufficient for 4-hour windows
- Month 3: CEO reports feeling "like a different person" (most common feedback)
- Month 6: Company trajectory visibly shifts because strategic work is actually happening

### How This Prevents the Firefighter CEO Pattern
Firefighting is fundamentally a Manager-mode activity. When a CEO lives entirely in Manager mode, every problem requires their real-time attention. By protecting Maker blocks, the CEO forces the organization to solve problems without them for half the day, which builds team capability AND ensures the strategic work that prevents future fires actually gets done.

# 4. TIME-BLOCKING METHODOLOGIES

## System A: Cal Newport's Deep Work Time-Blocking

**Source:** Cal Newport, "Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World" (2016) and "The Time-Block Planner" (2020).

**Evidence Base:** Newport's research at Georgetown University demonstrates that time-blockers accomplish roughly **twice as much work per week** compared to those using reactive methods, with significantly reduced professional stress and anxiety. Used by Elon Musk, Bill Gates, and thousands of documented high performers.

### The Core Method

**Every minute of your work day gets assigned to a block.** There is no unplanned time. Even "catch-up" or "flexible" time is explicitly blocked.

### CEO-Specific Implementation: The Journalistic Approach

Newport identifies four Deep Work scheduling strategies. For CEOs with unpredictable schedules, the **Journalistic Approach** is recommended:

> "Take advantage of any available block of free time in the calendar to schedule focused work sessions."

This differs from the Monastic (full isolation) or Bimodal (seasonal retreats) approaches because it works within the reality of a CEO's day.

### Step-by-Step Daily Implementation

**The Night Before (10 minutes):**
1. Open your calendar for tomorrow
2. Identify every pre-scheduled meeting and commitment
3. In the remaining gaps, assign specific deep work tasks
4. Block at least one 90-minute uninterrupted deep work session (morning preferred)
5. Assign shallow tasks (email, admin) to specific blocks (never "whenever")
6. Write your "Top 3" outcomes for tomorrow on a card or planner

**Each Morning (5 minutes):**
1. Review your time-blocked plan
2. Adjust for any overnight changes
3. Confirm your deep work blocks are protected
4. Set your phone to Do Not Disturb for the first deep work block

**During the Day (ongoing):**
1. Work within your blocks -- when a block ends, move to the next task
2. If a block runs over, adjust future blocks immediately (cross out and re-draw)
3. Batch all small tasks into designated "shallow work" blocks
4. Never check email outside of designated email blocks

**End of Day -- The Shutdown Ritual (15 minutes):**
1. Review your email inbox one final time; respond only to what is truly urgent
2. Transfer any incomplete tasks to your master task list
3. Review tomorrow's calendar and confirm your time blocks
4. Say "Shutdown complete" (Newport's literal practice) -- a verbal cue that work is done
5. Do not check email or work messages until your first scheduled block tomorrow

### Weekly Application
- **Sunday Evening (30 min):** Create your weekly plan -- assign theme/focus to each day, block recurring meetings, identify the week's top 3-5 priorities
- **Friday Afternoon (30 min):** Conduct the Weekly Review -- what got done, what didn't, what needs to carry forward, what can be eliminated

### The Deep Work Session Structure for CEOs (90-120 minutes)

```
Minute 0-5:    Review the specific problem/project you are working on
Minute 5-10:   Write down (on paper) what "done" looks like for this session
Minute 10-80:  Work without interruption. No phone, no email, no Slack.
Minute 80-85:  Capture notes on what was accomplished and next steps
Minute 85-90:  Brief break before next block
```

### Metrics & Success Criteria
- Track "Deep Work Hours" per week (target: 15-20 for SMB CEO)
- Track "Shallow Work Hours" per week (target: keep below 30% of total)
- Measure: How many times did you deviate from your time-block plan? (target: < 3 major deviations per day)
- Quality metric: Are strategic projects completing on time?

### Time Investment Required
- Daily: 30 minutes (15 min evening planning + 5 min morning review + 10 min adjustments)
- Weekly: 60 minutes (30 min Sunday planning + 30 min Friday review)
- Learning curve: 2 weeks to build the habit, 6 weeks to mastery

### Expected Impact Timeline
- Day 1: Immediate increase in intentionality
- Week 1: Noticeable reduction in "where did the day go?" feeling
- Week 2-3: First significant deep work outputs completed
- Month 2: Roughly 2x increase in meaningful output per week
- Month 3: Permanent shift in relationship with time

## System B: The Eisenhower Matrix Applied to CEO Weekly Planning

**Source:** Dwight D. Eisenhower's decision principle, formalized by Stephen Covey ("The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People"), and widely applied in modern productivity systems including Asana, Todoist, and Monday.com.

**Evidence Base:** The Eisenhower Matrix is one of the most researched and validated prioritization frameworks in management science. Covey's "Quadrant II" focus has been central to executive coaching for 35+ years.

### The Four Quadrants

```
                    URGENT                    NOT URGENT
                +-----------------------+-----------------------+
   IMPORTANT    |   QUADRANT 1          |   QUADRANT 2          |
                |   DO FIRST            |   SCHEDULE            |
                |                       |                       |
                |   - Client emergency  |   - Strategic planning |
                |   - Cash flow crisis  |   - Talent development |
                |   - Legal deadline    |   - System building    |
                |   - Safety issue      |   - Relationship invest|
                |   - Key employee quit |   - Personal growth    |
                |                       |   - Process improvement|
                +-----------------------+-----------------------+
   NOT          |   QUADRANT 3          |   QUADRANT 4          |
   IMPORTANT    |   DELEGATE            |   ELIMINATE            |
                |                       |                       |
                |   - Most emails       |   - Social media scroll|
                |   - Many meetings     |   - Unnecessary reports|
                |   - Some phone calls  |   - Perfectionism      |
                |   - Status updates    |   - Low-value meetings |
                |   - Routine approvals |   - Busywork           |
                +-----------------------+-----------------------+
```

### The CEO's Fatal Error

**Most SMB CEOs live in Quadrants 1 and 3** -- bouncing between genuine emergencies and tasks that feel urgent but are not actually important. Quadrant 2 -- the work that PREVENTS emergencies and BUILDS the future -- is chronically neglected.

**Covey's Core Insight:** The more time you invest in Quadrant 2, the smaller Quadrant 1 becomes. Every system you build, every person you develop, every process you improve ELIMINATES future emergencies. This is the escape from the firefighter trap.

### CEO Weekly Implementation Steps

**Step 1: The Sunday Brain Dump (15 minutes)**
1. Write down EVERYTHING on your mind -- tasks, worries, ideas, obligations
2. Get it ALL out of your head onto paper or a digital list
3. Do not organize yet -- just capture

**Step 2: The Quadrant Sort (15 minutes)**
4. Take each item and place it in one of the four quadrants
5. Be ruthlessly honest: Is this TRULY important, or does it just feel urgent?
6. If you cannot explain why something is important to the company's 3-year vision, it is not Quadrant 1 or 2

**Step 3: The Quadrant Actions (15 minutes)**
7. **Q1 items:** Schedule these FIRST into your calendar for Monday/Tuesday
8. **Q2 items:** Block PROTECTED time for these (this is the most important step) -- put them into your Deep Work/Maker blocks
9. **Q3 items:** Delegate with a clear owner, deadline, and definition of done
10. **Q4 items:** Delete them. Literally remove them from your list. Say no.

**Step 4: The Q2 Protection Rule**
11. Before accepting any new meeting or commitment during the week, ask: "Does this serve Quadrant 2?"
12. If no, it goes to office hours, gets delegated, or gets eliminated
13. Your Quadrant 2 blocks are as non-negotiable as a meeting with your largest investor

### Daily Application
- **Morning (2 min):** Glance at your Eisenhower Matrix. Ask: "What is the single most important Q2 task I can advance today?"
- **Before each decision (10 sec):** Ask: "Is this urgent, important, both, or neither?"
- **Evening (5 min):** Review: "How much time did I spend in each quadrant today?" Write the percentages down.

### Weekly Application
- **Sunday Planning:** Sort all tasks into quadrants (as described above)
- **Wednesday Check-in:** Mid-week audit -- am I still protecting Q2 time?
- **Friday Review:** Calculate percentage of time in each quadrant. Target: 40%+ in Q2, <20% in Q1, <15% in Q3, <5% in Q4

### Target Quadrant Distribution for SMB CEOs

| Quadrant | Target % | Current Reality (Typical) |
|----------|---------|--------------------------|
| Q1: Urgent + Important | 15-20% | 35-45% |
| Q2: Not Urgent + Important | 40-50% | 5-15% |
| Q3: Urgent + Not Important | 15-20% | 25-35% |
| Q4: Not Urgent + Not Important | 5-10% | 10-20% |

### How This Prevents the Firefighter CEO Pattern
The Eisenhower Matrix makes the cost of firefighting visible and measurable. When a CEO can see that they spent 45% of their week in Q1 (reactive emergencies) and only 10% in Q2 (prevention), the structural problem becomes undeniable. More importantly, every hour invested in Q2 work (building systems, developing people, improving processes) directly reduces the number of Q1 emergencies in future weeks. This is the mathematical escape from the firefighter trap.

# 5. THE CEO DAY CONCEPT

## System: Protected Strategic Thinking Day

**Source:** Composite framework drawn from: Solution Integrators ("What is a CEO Day?"), Susan Jensen ("Grow Your Business with a Monthly CEO Day"), The CEO Collective ("The CEO Planner"), and The Alternative Board ("Strategy Planning Day: 6 Key Elements").

**Evidence Base:** Organizations with intentional CEO strategic time outperform those without. HBR research shows CEOs need "larger blocks of time away from the office" for strategic preparation. BCG recommends 2-3 hours of strategic reflection twice per week at minimum.

### Definition

A **CEO Day** is a designated block of time (half-day minimum, full day recommended) with **zero meetings, zero calls, zero operational interruptions**, dedicated exclusively to strategic thinking, planning, and high-leverage decision-making.

### Recommended Frequency

| Company Stage | Recommended CEO Day Frequency | Duration |
|---------------|-------------------------------|----------|
| $1M-$3M | Minimum 1 half-day per week | 4 hours |
| $3M-$10M | 1 full day per week + 1 monthly deep dive | 8 hours weekly, 1 full day monthly offsite |
| $10M-$25M | 1 full day per week + quarterly 2-day offsite | 8 hours weekly |
| $25M-$50M | 2 half-days per week + quarterly 2-day offsite | 8 hours weekly |

```

               - Partnership and M&A opportunity evaluation

10:15 - 12:00  STRATEGIC DEEP WORK BLOCK 2

               - Read a business book or industry report
               - Listen to a relevant podcast
               - Review competitor announcements

1:00 - 2:30    PEOPLE & TALENT STRATEGY

               - Write development plans for your top 3 leaders
```

### Implementation Steps

**Step 1: Choose Your Day (This Week)**
- Wednesday works best for most CEOs (avoids Monday fire drills and Friday wind-down)
- If a full day is impossible, start with Wednesday morning (8 AM - 12 PM)
- Block it on your calendar as "CEO Strategic Day -- NO MEETINGS" recurring weekly

**Step 2: Communicate the Boundary (This Week)**
- Tell your leadership team: "Wednesday is my strategic thinking day. I will not be available for meetings or calls unless there is a genuine emergency."
- Define "emergency" explicitly: imminent revenue loss > $X, safety/legal issue, or key employee separation
- Appoint a decision-maker for your absence (COO, VP, or senior leader)

**Step 3: Leave the Building (Week 1)**
- Physical separation from the office eliminates 80% of interruptions
- Go to a location with no employees, no office phone, no walk-ins
- Bring: laptop, notebook, your strategic planning documents, one business book
- Do NOT bring: anything that connects you to operational issues

**Step 4: Create Your CEO Day Agenda Template (Week 1)**
- Use the sample agenda above as a starting point
- Customize based on your company's current priorities
- Print it out and bring it with you -- do not rely on willpower to stay on track

**Step 5: Debrief and Capture (Every CEO Day)**
- Last 30 minutes: document every decision, insight, and action item
- Share relevant decisions with your leadership team the next morning
- Track: "What is the single most valuable output from today's CEO Day?"

### Metrics & Success Criteria
- Output metric: At least 1 significant strategic decision or document per CEO Day
- Quality metric: Leadership team can identify specific business improvements that resulted from CEO Day outputs
- Consistency metric: CEO Day happens 48+ weeks per year (allow 4 weeks of travel/exceptions)
- Team metric: Zero operational emergencies escalated to CEO during CEO Day (target after Month 3)

### Time Investment Required
- 4-8 hours per week (the time already exists -- it is currently consumed by low-value activities)
- Location change: 30 minutes of commute (invested in thinking time)
- Setup: 1 hour (calendar blocking, team communication, agenda template)

### Expected Impact Timeline
- CEO Day 1: Relief and clarity ("I forgot what thinking feels like")
- Month 1: First strategic insight that changes a meaningful business decision
- Month 2: Team begins solving problems independently during your CEO Day
- Month 3: Visible acceleration in strategic project completion
- Month 6: CEO Day outputs are driving company direction; return to firefighting feels impossible

### How This Prevents the Firefighter CEO Pattern
The CEO Day is the **single most powerful anti-firefighting tool** because it creates guaranteed strategic capacity. A firefighter CEO has zero time to ask "Why do fires keep starting?" The CEO Day creates that time. After 4-6 weeks, the insights from CEO Days begin reducing the fire frequency. After 3-6 months, the CEO has built enough systems, hired enough talent, and improved enough processes that the fires genuinely decrease.

# 6. JEFF BEZOS DECISION FRAMEWORKS FOR SMBS

## System A: Type 1 vs. Type 2 Decisions (One-Way vs. Two-Way Doors)

**Source:** Jeff Bezos, 2015 Amazon Shareholder Letter. Further elaborated in subsequent shareholder letters and interviews.

**Evidence Base:** This framework is credited as one of the key drivers of Amazon's speed and innovation, enabling a company with 1.5M+ employees to make decisions with startup-like velocity. Widely adopted across Silicon Valley and increasingly in mid-market companies.

### The Framework

**Type 1 Decisions (One-Way Doors):**
- Irreversible or nearly irreversible
- High consequence
- Long-term commitment
- Examples: Acquiring a company, signing a 10-year lease, choosing a new market to enter, hiring a C-suite executive, taking on significant debt

> Bezos: "These decisions must be made methodically, carefully, slowly, with great deliberation and consultation."

**Type 2 Decisions (Two-Way Doors):**
- Reversible
- Low-to-moderate consequence
- Can be changed if wrong
- Examples: Pricing changes, marketing campaigns, new product features, vendor selection, process changes, hiring for non-leadership roles

> Bezos: "If you've made a suboptimal Type 2 decision, you don't have to live with the consequences for that long. You can reopen the door and go back through."

### The Critical Insight for SMB CEOs

**The problem Bezos identified:** As organizations grow, they tend to use the heavyweight Type 1 decision process for EVERYTHING -- including Type 2 decisions. This creates:
- Slowness (decisions that should take 1 day take 3 weeks)
- Excessive risk aversion (every change feels permanent)
- Failure to experiment (no one will try anything because "we need more data")
- CEO bottleneck (everything requires CEO approval)

**For SMB CEOs specifically:** The founder often makes ALL decisions like Type 1 decisions because they built the company and every decision "feels" consequential. This is the core of the bottleneck problem.

### Exact Implementation Steps

**Step 1: Categorize Your Pending Decisions (This Week)**
1. List every decision currently waiting for your input
2. For each one, ask: "If this decision is wrong, can I reverse it within 90 days for less than $X?" (Set X as 2% of annual revenue)
3. If yes: It is Type 2. Move to Step 2.
4. If no: It is Type 1. Move to Step 3.

**Step 2: For Type 2 Decisions -- DELEGATE AND ACCELERATE**
5. Identify who on your team could make this decision
6. Give them the decision authority with one rule: "Make the best decision you can with available information. If it doesn't work, we'll reverse it. You do not need my approval."
7. Set a **70% information threshold** (Bezos rule): If you have 70% of the information you wish you had, decide now. Waiting for 90% is too slow.
8. **Same-day decisions:** All Type 2 decisions should be made the day they surface, not next week.

**Step 3: For Type 1 Decisions -- USE THE FULL PROCESS**
9. Write a decision memo (see 6-Page Memo below) OR at minimum a 1-page brief answering:
   - What is the decision?
   - What are the options?
   - What are the risks and second-order consequences of each option?
   - What would have to be true for each option to be the right choice?
   - What is the recommendation and why?
10. Gather input from 2-3 trusted advisors (not 10 people -- see Two-Pizza Rule)
11. Set a decision deadline (even Type 1 decisions should not linger indefinitely)
12. Decide. Commit. Communicate widely.

### Daily Application
- **Morning Decision Sort:** When decisions arrive during the day, spend 10 seconds categorizing: "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door?" If two-way, delegate or decide immediately. If one-way, schedule dedicated thinking time.
- **End of Day:** Review: "How many Type 2 decisions did I make today that should have been delegated? How many am I still sitting on?"

### Weekly Application
- **Monday:** Review all pending decisions with leadership team. Categorize as Type 1 or Type 2 collectively.
- **During L10 Meeting:** Apply Type 1/Type 2 filter during IDS session -- most issues in the meeting are Type 2 and can be resolved in the meeting itself.

### SMB Decision Speed Targets

| Decision Type | Target Turnaround | Current Reality (Typical SMB) |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Type 2, Small ($<5K impact) | Same day | 1-2 weeks |
| Type 2, Medium ($5K-$50K impact) | 1-3 days | 2-4 weeks |
| Type 1, Small ($50K-$250K impact) | 1-2 weeks | 1-3 months |
| Type 1, Large ($250K+ impact) | 2-4 weeks | 3-6 months |

## System B: The Two-Pizza Rule

**Source:** Jeff Bezos, implemented at Amazon from the earliest days. Documented in Brad Stone's "The Everything Store" and numerous Amazon leadership principles.

**Evidence Base:** Amazon's consistent innovation across dozens of business lines is attributed in part to small team structure. Research by Ringelmann (social loafing), Dunbar (cognitive limits), and Hackman (team effectiveness) all support the principle that smaller teams outperform larger ones for most tasks.

### The Rule

**No meeting should include more people than can be fed by two large pizzas** (typically 5-8 people, Bezos prefers 2-5).

### Why It Matters for SMBs

| Meeting Size | Typical Outcome | Bezos Principle |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 2-3 people | Fast decisions, clear ownership, honest debate | Ideal for most decisions |
| 4-6 people | Productive with clear facilitation | Maximum for strategic discussions |
| 7-10 people | Groupthink risk, social loafing, slow decisions | Too large for decision-making |
| 10+ people | Information broadcast only | Use email or async update instead |

### SMB CEO Implementation Steps

**Step 1: Meeting Audit (This Week)**
1. List every recurring meeting on your calendar
2. For each: How many attendees? What decisions are being made?
3. Flag any meeting with > 6 people that is trying to make decisions

**Step 2: Right-Size Meetings (Week 2)**
4. For each flagged meeting, ask: "Who are the 2-3 people who actually need to be in this room to make this decision?"
5. Invite only those people. Everyone else gets the decision communicated via email/Slack afterward.
6. Rule of thumb: If someone is in the meeting "just to stay informed," they should not be in the meeting.

**Step 3: Two-Pizza Teams for Projects (Ongoing)**
7. When assigning project teams, cap at 5-6 people maximum
8. Each team has a clear owner (single-threaded leader)
9. Teams have full decision authority within their scope (Type 2 decisions, per above)

### Metrics
- Average meeting size across all recurring meetings (target: < 5)
- Percentage of meetings where a decision was actually made (target: > 80%)
- Total hours per week in meetings (target: < 20 for CEO)

## System C: The 6-Page Memo (Narrative Decision Document)

**Source:** Jeff Bezos, implemented at Amazon in 2004 when he banned PowerPoint from executive meetings.

**Evidence Base:** Amazon credits the 6-page narrative memo as one of the "smartest things we ever did." Used for every major decision at Amazon for 20+ years. Forces clear thinking and eliminates the persuasive-but-shallow quality of slide decks.

### The Format

Bezos requires Amazon executives to write a 6-page narratively structured memo for important decisions. At the start of each meeting, everyone spends 30 minutes reading the memo in silence ("study hall"), then the remaining time is discussion.

### Structure of the Memo

```
PAGE 1: THE CONTEXT
- What is the current situation?
- What problem are we solving?
- Why does this matter now?

PAGE 2-3: THE PROPOSAL
- What is the specific recommendation?
- How will it work?
- What is the timeline?
- What resources are required?

PAGE 4: THE CUSTOMER/STAKEHOLDER IMPACT
- How does this affect our customers?
- How does this affect our employees?
- How does this affect our financials?

PAGE 5: THE RISKS AND MITIGATIONS
- What could go wrong?
- What are the second-order consequences?
- How do we mitigate each risk?
- What is the worst-case scenario?

PAGE 6: THE FAQ
- Anticipated questions and answers
- Objections and responses
- Metrics for success
- Decision timeline
```

### SMB CEO Adaptation: The 2-Page Decision Brief

For SMBs, a full 6-page memo is often overkill. A simplified 2-page version:

```
PAGE 1: SITUATION + RECOMMENDATION
- Current situation (3-4 sentences)
- The specific decision to be made (1 sentence)
- Recommendation and rationale (1 paragraph)
- Options considered and why they were rejected (bullet points)

PAGE 2: IMPACT + RISKS + NEXT STEPS
- Financial impact (revenue, cost, cash flow)
- People impact (hiring, roles, culture)
- Customer impact
- Top 3 risks and how we mitigate each
- Decision deadline
- If approved, first 3 actions and owners
```

### Implementation Steps for SMBs
1. Write one 2-page Decision Brief for your next major decision
2. Share it with your leadership team 24 hours before the meeting
3. At the meeting, spend the first 10 minutes reading it together in silence
4. Then discuss. The author takes notes on questions and objections.
5. Decide in the meeting or set a clear decision deadline.

### How This Prevents the Firefighter CEO Pattern
All three Bezos frameworks attack the firefighting pattern at the root:
- **Type 1/Type 2** prevents decision bottlenecks by pushing 80% of decisions down to the team
- **Two-Pizza Rule** prevents bloated meetings that waste CEO time
- **6-Page Memo** prevents sloppy thinking that leads to bad decisions that create future crises

### Morning Ritual (Every Work Day, 15 minutes)

```
1. Review your "Top 3" for the day (written the night before)
2. Check calendar: Confirm Maker vs. Manager blocks
3. Identify the single most important Quadrant 2 task for today
4. Set phone to Do Not Disturb for first Maker block
5. Begin work (no email first -- start with your #1 priority)
```

### Evening Shutdown Ritual (Every Work Day, 15 minutes)

```
1. Review inbox -- respond to truly urgent items only (2 min)
2. Update task list -- capture all open loops (3 min)
3. Review tomorrow's calendar and time blocks (3 min)
4. Write tomorrow's "Top 3" priorities (2 min)
5. Confirm tomorrow's day type: Focus, Buffer, or Free (1 min)
6. Clear desk/close laptop (2 min)
7. Say "Shutdown complete" -- work is over (2 min transition)
```

### The Anti-Firefighting Cascade

Each framework contributes to eliminating the reactive CEO pattern:

```
LAYER 1: PREVENTION (Quadrant 2 / CEO Day)
  Strategic thinking prevents future crises
  System building eliminates recurring problems
  Talent development creates leaders who handle problems without you
  |
  v
LAYER 2: STRUCTURE (Sullivan's Day Types / Maker-Manager Split)
  Focus Days guarantee strategic work happens even when fires exist
  Buffer Days contain reactive work to designated windows
  Free Days prevent burnout (exhausted CEOs make everything a crisis)
  |
  v
LAYER 3: EXECUTION (Time Blocking / L10 Meetings)
  Every minute has a purpose -- no time leaks to low-value activities
  L10 Meeting creates a weekly pressure valve for issues
  Shutdown ritual prevents work from invading recovery time
  |
  v
LAYER 4: DECISION SPEED (Bezos Frameworks)
  Type 1/Type 2 sorting prevents decision bottlenecks
  Two-Pizza Rule prevents bloated, slow meetings
  Narrative memos prevent sloppy decisions that create future fires
  |
  v
RESULT: CEO operates at 60-65% proactive, strategic capacity
  (vs. industry average of 43% per HBS data, and typical
  SMB reality of 10-25%)
```

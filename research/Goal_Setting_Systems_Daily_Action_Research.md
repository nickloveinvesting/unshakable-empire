# SECTION 1: OKR SETTING FOR QUARTERLY GOALS --> WEEKLY KEY RESULTS --> DAILY ACTIVITIES

## 1.1 The OKR Format (Objective + 3-5 Key Results)

### What OKRs Are

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) is a goal-setting framework originally developed at Intel by Andy Grove and popularized by John Doerr at Google. The format pairs a qualitative, inspirational Objective with 3-5 quantitative, measurable Key Results.

### The Formula

```
OBJECTIVE: A qualitative, inspirational statement of WHAT you want to achieve
  KR1: A measurable outcome (metric + target + deadline)
  KR2: A measurable outcome (metric + target + deadline)
  KR3: A measurable outcome (metric + target + deadline)
  [KR4: Optional]
  [KR5: Optional]
```

### Rules for Writing Objectives
- Must be qualitative (no numbers in the Objective itself)
- Must be inspirational and motivating
- Must be achievable within the quarter (for quarterly OKRs)
- Should answer: "Where do I want to go?"
- Should be action-oriented, starting with a verb

### Rules for Writing Key Results
- Must be quantitative and measurable (include a number)
- Must be outcomes, NOT activities or tasks
- Must have a clear success threshold
- Should answer: "How will I know I got there?"
- Aim for 3-5 per Objective (3 recommended for small businesses)
- Mix of quantity, quality, and efficiency metrics when possible

### OKR Template

```
OBJECTIVE: [Verb] + [What you want to achieve] + [Why it matters]

  KR1: [Increase/Decrease/Maintain] [metric] from [X] to [Y] by [date]
  KR2: [Achieve/Complete/Launch] [specific deliverable] by [date]
  KR3: [Reach] [target number] of [measurable thing] by [date]

INITIATIVES (the work you do to move Key Results):
  - Initiative A: [specific project or task]
  - Initiative B: [specific project or task]
  - Initiative C: [specific project or task]
```

## 1.2 Cascading OKRs: Company --> Department --> Individual

### The Cascade Model

Cascading is the process by which top-level company OKRs flow downward so that department heads, managers, and individual employees take ownership of specific Key Results. Each level's Key Results become the next level's Objectives.

### Three-Level Cascade Structure

```
LEVEL 1: COMPANY OKRs (Set by CEO/Leadership Team)
  Objective: Become the market leader in our region
    KR1: Grow revenue from $2M to $3M
    KR2: Achieve Net Promoter Score of 70+
    KR3: Expand to 3 new geographic markets

        |
        v

LEVEL 2: DEPARTMENT OKRs (Set by Department Heads, aligned to Company KRs)

  SALES DEPARTMENT (aligned to Company KR1: Grow revenue)
    Objective: Build a high-performing sales engine
      KR1: Close $750K in new business per quarter
      KR2: Increase average deal size from $15K to $22K
      KR3: Reduce sales cycle from 45 days to 30 days

  MARKETING DEPARTMENT (aligned to Company KR3: Expand markets)
    Objective: Generate qualified demand in 3 new markets
      KR1: Generate 200 marketing-qualified leads per month from new markets
      KR2: Achieve 3% website conversion rate from new market traffic
      KR3: Launch 3 market-specific campaigns with $50K+ pipeline each

        |
        v

LEVEL 3: INDIVIDUAL OKRs (Set by team members, aligned to Department KRs)

  SALES REP (aligned to Dept KR1: Close $750K)
    Objective: Consistently exceed my sales targets
      KR1: Close $190K in new business this quarter
      KR2: Maintain 30%+ close rate on qualified opportunities
      KR3: Generate 15 self-sourced qualified leads per month
```

### Alignment vs. Strict Cascade

Modern OKR thinking favors alignment over strict cascading. Rather than mandating that every individual KR maps to a department KR, teams define their own OKRs that clearly support the company's priorities. The distinction:

- **Strict Cascade**: Company KR becomes Department Objective (rigid, slow, time-consuming)
- **Alignment**: Teams write their own OKRs that clearly contribute to company priorities (flexible, faster, more ownership)

**Recommendation for small businesses**: Use alignment rather than strict cascading. Have teams propose their OKRs, then verify alignment with leadership.

## 1.3 Weekly Check-In Format for OKR Progress

### The 15-30 Minute Weekly OKR Check-In

```
WEEKLY OKR CHECK-IN AGENDA (15-30 minutes)

1. WINS & HIGHLIGHTS (5 min)
   - Each person shares one win from last week
   - Celebrate small victories

2. OKR PROGRESS UPDATE (10 min)
   - Each KR owner provides:
     * Current score (0.0-1.0)
     * Confidence level (Red / Yellow / Green)
     * What moved the needle this week
     * What's blocking progress
   - Timebox: 2-3 minutes per person
   - RULE: Dashboards must be updated BEFORE the meeting

3. PRIORITIES FOR THIS WEEK (10 min)
   - Each person states their top 1-3 priorities for the week
   - Priorities must connect to a Key Result
   - Format: "This week I will [action] to move [KR#] from [X] to [Y]"

4. BLOCKERS & REQUESTS (5 min)
   - Surface any obstacles
   - Make specific asks for help
   - Assign owners to resolve blockers
```

### Confidence Scoring (Traffic Light System)

Use this alongside the 0.0-1.0 score to give a forward-looking assessment:

| Color  | Meaning                                      | Action Required                   |
|--------|----------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| GREEN  | On track to hit target                       | Continue current approach         |
| YELLOW | At risk; may miss without changes            | Discuss adjustments in check-in   |
| RED    | Off track; will miss without intervention    | Escalate; problem-solve this week |

## 1.4 How to Score OKRs (The 0.0-1.0 Scale)

### Google's OKR Scoring System

Google uses a 0.0 to 1.0 scale where each Key Result is scored individually, and the Objective score is the average of its Key Results.

```
SCORING SCALE:

  0.0 - 0.3  = RED    --> "We failed to make significant progress"
  0.4 - 0.6  = YELLOW --> "We made progress but fell short of completion"
  0.7 - 1.0  = GREEN  --> "We delivered"

THE SWEET SPOT:

  For ASPIRATIONAL OKRs (stretch goals):
    0.6 - 0.7 = Success (the "sweet spot")
    Below 0.4 = Failed; goals may have been wrong or execution poor
    Above 0.7 consistently = Goals weren't ambitious enough

  For COMMITTED OKRs (must-achieve):
    1.0 = Expected (these are promises, not aspirations)
    Below 1.0 = Requires root-cause analysis
```

### Scoring Template

```
END-OF-QUARTER OKR SCORECARD

Objective: [Objective Statement]
Overall Score: [Average of KR scores]

  KR1: [Key Result Statement]
    Target: [what you aimed for]
    Actual: [what you achieved]
    Score: [0.0 - 1.0]
    Notes: [what worked, what didn't]

  KR2: [Key Result Statement]
    Target: [what you aimed for]
    Actual: [what you achieved]
    Score: [0.0 - 1.0]
    Notes: [what worked, what didn't]

  KR3: [Key Result Statement]
    Target: [what you aimed for]
    Actual: [what you achieved]
    Score: [0.0 - 1.0]
    Notes: [what worked, what didn't]

REFLECTION:
  - What drove our strongest results?
  - What inhibited progress?
  - What will we do differently next quarter?
  - Should this Objective continue, evolve, or be retired?
```

### What 0.7 Actually Means

A score of 0.7 on an aspirational OKR means: "We achieved 70% of an intentionally ambitious target." Because aspirational OKRs are designed to be stretch goals (only 50-70% likely to be fully achieved), hitting 0.7 means the team pushed hard, made significant progress, and nearly reached an ambitious target. This is considered a strong result.

## 1.5 Common OKR Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: Writing Activities as Key Results
```
BAD:  KR: "Launch email marketing campaign"
      (This is a task, not a result)

GOOD: KR: "Increase email subscriber conversion rate from 2% to 5%"
      (This is a measurable outcome)
```

### Mistake 2: Setting Too Many OKRs
```
BAD:  5 Objectives with 5 Key Results each = 25 things to track
      (Diluted focus, nothing gets done well)

GOOD: 2-3 Objectives with 3 Key Results each = 6-9 things to track
      (Focused effort, meaningful progress)
```

### Mistake 3: Sandbagging (Setting Easy Goals)
```
BAD:  KR: "Grow revenue by 2%" (when 2% is already happening organically)
      (If you're consistently scoring 1.0, your goals are too easy)

GOOD: KR: "Grow revenue by 25%" (ambitious but possible with great execution)
      (0.7 would mean 17.5% growth, which is still excellent)
```

### Mistake 4: Tying OKRs to Compensation
```
BAD:  "Hit your OKRs or lose your bonus"
      (Creates sandbagging, fear, and gaming the system)

GOOD: OKRs for alignment and ambition; separate KPIs for compensation
      (Allows genuine stretch without fear)
```

### Mistake 5: Set-and-Forget
```
BAD:  Set OKRs in January, check them in March
      (No course correction, no learning, no urgency)

GOOD: Weekly check-ins, mid-quarter adjustment if needed
      (Continuous alignment, early problem detection)
```

### Mistake 6: No Connection Between OKRs and Daily Work
```
BAD:  Beautiful OKRs that sit in a spreadsheet no one opens
      (OKRs become a compliance exercise)

GOOD: Every weekly priority and daily task connects to a Key Result
      (OKRs become the operating system for work)
```

## 1.6 Good vs. Bad OKRs for Small Business

### Example 1: Service Business

```
BAD OKR:
  Objective: Make our clients feel good when they do business with us
  KR1: Motivate employees to enhance customer service
  KR2: Make customers happy
  KR3: Improve our reputation
  PROBLEM: Vague, unmeasurable, no targets, no deadlines

GOOD OKR:
  Objective: Deliver a level of customer service no big company can match
  KR1: Achieve Net Promoter Score of 75+ (currently 52)
  KR2: Reduce average customer response time from 4 hours to 30 minutes
  KR3: Get 25 five-star Google reviews from customers this quarter
  KR4: Resolve 95% of customer issues on first contact (currently 71%)
  WHY IT WORKS: Specific metrics, clear targets, measurable progress
```

### Example 2: E-Commerce Business

```
BAD OKR:
  Objective: Sell more products online
  KR1: Get more website traffic
  KR2: Run social media ads
  KR3: Update the website
  PROBLEM: No numbers, activities listed as results

GOOD OKR:
  Objective: Build a profitable, scalable online sales engine
  KR1: Increase monthly online revenue from $45K to $75K
  KR2: Improve website conversion rate from 1.8% to 3.2%
  KR3: Reduce customer acquisition cost from $42 to $28
  WHY IT WORKS: Profit-focused, has baseline AND target, measures outcomes
```

### Example 3: Professional Services Firm

```
BAD OKR:
  Objective: Grow the business
  KR1: Get more clients
  KR2: Do better marketing
  KR3: Hire more people
  PROBLEM: No specificity, no numbers, "more" is not a target

GOOD OKR:
  Objective: Establish a predictable client acquisition system
  KR1: Generate 40 qualified discovery calls per month (currently 12)
  KR2: Achieve 35% close rate on proposals (currently 20%)
  KR3: Build a pipeline of $500K in qualified opportunities
  WHY IT WORKS: Addresses the system, not just the result; specific numbers
```

# SECTION 2: THE "12 WEEK YEAR" (Brian Moran)

## 2.1 Why Annual Goals Fail and 12-Week Cycles Work

### The Problem with Annual Planning

Brian Moran identified that most people and organizations don't lack ideas but struggle with effective execution. The core issue with annual goals:

1. **End-of-year urgency**: People coast for 9 months, then scramble in October-December
2. **False sense of time**: "I have 12 months" creates complacency
3. **Delayed feedback**: You don't know you're off track until it's too late
4. **Planning fatigue**: Annual plans become outdated within weeks
5. **Lack of urgency**: Every day feels low-stakes when you have 365 of them

### Why 12 Weeks Works

By compressing the "year" to 12 weeks:
- **Urgency is constant**: Every week is the equivalent of a month in a traditional year
- **There's no "someday"**: Week 1 of 12 creates immediate accountability
- **Feedback is fast**: You know by Week 4 if you're on track
- **Planning stays relevant**: 12-week plans rarely become outdated
- **Focus intensifies**: You can only fit your most important goals into 12 weeks
- **You get 4 "years" per calendar year**: 4 fresh starts, 4 chances to adjust

## 2.2 The 12-Week Plan Format

### Step 1: Set Your 12-Week Goals (Maximum 3)

```
12-WEEK GOAL TEMPLATE

Goal 1: [Specific, measurable outcome to achieve by Week 12]
  Current State: [Where you are now]
  Target State: [Where you want to be]
  Why This Matters: [Connection to annual/long-term vision]

Goal 2: [Specific, measurable outcome to achieve by Week 12]
  Current State: [Where you are now]
  Target State: [Where you want to be]
  Why This Matters: [Connection to annual/long-term vision]

Goal 3: [Specific, measurable outcome to achieve by Week 12]
  Current State: [Where you are now]
  Target State: [Where you want to be]
  Why This Matters: [Connection to annual/long-term vision]
```

### Step 2: Define Weekly Tactics for Each Goal

For each goal, identify the specific, recurring weekly actions (tactics) that will drive results.

```
12-WEEK TACTICAL PLAN

GOAL 1: Close $250K in new revenue

  Week 1:  [ ] Make 25 outbound calls  [ ] Send 15 proposals  [ ] Follow up on 10 pending deals
  Week 2:  [ ] Make 25 outbound calls  [ ] Send 15 proposals  [ ] Follow up on 10 pending deals
  Week 3:  [ ] Make 25 outbound calls  [ ] Send 15 proposals  [ ] Conduct 3 discovery meetings
  Week 4:  [ ] Make 25 outbound calls  [ ] Send 15 proposals  [ ] Follow up on 10 pending deals
  ...
  Week 12: [ ] Make 25 outbound calls  [ ] Send 15 proposals  [ ] Close remaining pipeline

GOAL 2: Launch new service offering

  Week 1:  [ ] Complete market research  [ ] Draft service description  [ ] Identify 5 beta clients
  Week 2:  [ ] Build pricing model  [ ] Create sales collateral  [ ] Reach out to beta clients
  Week 3:  [ ] Finalize offering  [ ] Train team  [ ] Onboard first beta client
  ...
```

### Step 3: Create Weekly Plans

Each Monday (or Friday for the following week), create a specific plan:

```

  [ ] Tactic A (due: day)
  [ ] Tactic B (due: day)

GOAL 3 TACTICS THIS WEEK:
  [ ] Tactic A (due: day)
  [ ] Tactic B (due: day)

TOTAL PLANNED TACTICS: [#]
```

## 2.3 Weekly Scoring System (85% Execution = Success)

### The Weekly Execution Score

The single most important metric in the 12 Week Year is the Weekly Execution Score. It measures the percentage of planned tactics you completed.

```
WEEKLY EXECUTION SCORE FORMULA:

  Execution Score = (Tactics Completed / Tactics Planned) x 100

EXAMPLE:
  Planned Tactics: 20
  Completed Tactics: 17
  Execution Score: 17/20 = 85%

SCORING BENCHMARKS:
  90-100%  = Exceptional execution (verify goals are ambitious enough)
  85-89%   = SUPERB (Moran's target; this level of execution nearly guarantees goal achievement)
  70-84%   = Good but needs improvement
  Below 70% = Execution breakdown; requires immediate correction
```

### Why 85% and Not 100%

Moran found through research that 85% completion of weekly tactics is the threshold at which goal achievement becomes highly probable. The 15% buffer accounts for:
- Legitimate emergencies and interruptions
- Tactics that turn out to be unnecessary
- Learning and adjustment

Consistently hitting 100% may indicate tactics are too easy or too few.

### Weekly Scorecard Template

```
WEEKLY SCORECARD - WEEK [#] of 12

                    PLANNED    COMPLETED    SCORE
Goal 1 Tactics:     [8]        [7]          87.5%
Goal 2 Tactics:     [6]        [5]          83.3%
Goal 3 Tactics:     [6]        [5]          83.3%
----------------------------------------------
TOTAL:              [20]       [17]         85.0%

LEAD INDICATORS:
  - Calls made: [#]
  - Proposals sent: [#]
  - Meetings held: [#]

LAG INDICATORS:
  - Revenue closed: [$]
  - Deals in pipeline: [#]
  - Client satisfaction: [score]

REFLECTION:
  - What went well this week?
  - What tactics did I miss and why?
  - What will I do differently next week?
```

## 2.4 The 13th Week (Review/Planning Week)

### Purpose

The 13th week is a dedicated buffer/transition week between 12-week cycles. It serves three purposes:

1. **Celebrate and Reflect**: Score the completed 12-week cycle
2. **Recover**: Catch up on anything that slipped; decompress
3. **Plan**: Design the next 12-week cycle

### 13th Week Agenda

```
13TH WEEK SCHEDULE

DAY 1-2: REVIEW & SCORE
  - Calculate final 12-week execution scores
  - Score each goal (achieved / not achieved / partially achieved)
  - Review weekly execution scores trend
  - Document lessons learned
  - Celebrate wins (this is non-negotiable)

DAY 3: STRATEGIC THINKING
  - Review annual vision: Are we still on track?
  - Identify what changed in the business environment
  - Determine which goals should continue, evolve, or be replaced
  - Brainstorm new opportunities

DAY 4-5: PLAN THE NEXT 12 WEEKS
  - Set 1-3 new 12-week goals
  - Define weekly tactics for each goal
  - Build the tactical plan (all 12 weeks mapped out)
  - Create the Week 1 plan in detail
  - Set up tracking systems
```

### 12-Week Cycle Calendar (Annual View)

```
CALENDAR YEAR BREAKDOWN:

  Cycle 1: Weeks 1-12   (Jan 1 - Mar 25)     --> 13th Week: Mar 26-31
  Cycle 2: Weeks 1-12   (Apr 1 - Jun 24)      --> 13th Week: Jun 25-30
  Cycle 3: Weeks 1-12   (Jul 1 - Sep 23)      --> 13th Week: Sep 24-30
  Cycle 4: Weeks 1-12   (Oct 1 - Dec 24)      --> 13th Week: Dec 25-31

  Total: 4 complete cycles + 4 review/planning weeks = 52 weeks
```

## 2.5 How to Structure Weekly Accountability

### Weekly Accountability Meeting (WAM)

The WAM is a peer accountability meeting (not a manager-subordinate meeting). It involves 2-4 people who commit to support, challenge, and encourage one another.

```
WEEKLY ACCOUNTABILITY MEETING FORMAT (30 minutes)

1. REPORT OUT (10 min)
   Each person shares:
   - Execution score for the week
   - What they completed
   - What they missed and why
   - Key results/wins

2. FEEDBACK & COACHING (10 min)
   - Peers ask clarifying questions
   - Share successful techniques
   - Offer suggestions for overcoming obstacles
   - Challenge each other constructively

3. INTENTIONS (10 min)
   Each person states:
   - Their execution goal for next week
   - The specific tactics they'll complete
   - Where they might need help
   - One thing they'll do differently

RULES:
  - Same time, same place, every week (non-negotiable)
  - No cancellations except genuine emergencies
  - Honesty over politeness
  - Focus on execution score, not just outcomes
```

## 2.6 Key Metrics: Execution Score, Lead/Lag Indicators

### Understanding Lead vs. Lag Indicators

```
LAG INDICATORS (Results - you can measure them but can't directly control them):
  - Revenue closed
  - Profit margin
  - Customer satisfaction score
  - Market share
  - Employee retention rate

LEAD INDICATORS (Activities - you can directly control them):
  - Number of sales calls made
  - Number of proposals sent
  - Customer touchpoints per week
  - Hours spent on strategic work
  - Content pieces published

THE RELATIONSHIP:
  Lead Indicators --> drive --> Lag Indicators
  (What you DO)              (What you GET)
```

### The Most Important Lead Indicator: Execution Score

Moran argues that the execution score is the most crucial lead indicator because it measures whether you're doing what you planned. If your plan is sound and you execute at 85%+, results follow.

### Performance Time Blocking

Moran prescribes three types of time blocks to protect execution:

```
TIME BLOCKING SYSTEM:

1. STRATEGIC BLOCKS (3 hours, weekly minimum)
   - Uninterrupted time working ON the business
   - No email, no phone, no interruptions
   - Focus on 12-week plan tactics
   - Schedule first thing Monday or Tuesday

2. BUFFER BLOCKS (30-60 minutes, 1-2x daily)
   - Handle email, voicemail, administrative tasks
   - Batch low-value activities into these windows
   - Prevents reactive work from consuming strategic time

3. BREAKOUT BLOCKS (3 hours, weekly minimum)
   - Time for rest, recreation, renewal
   - Mandatory -- not optional
   - Prevents burnout
   - At least one per week; ideally two
```

# SECTION 3: SMART GOALS CRITICISM -- BETTER ALTERNATIVES

## 3.1 What Research Says About SMART Goals Limitations

### SMART Goals Defined (for context)
- **S**pecific
- **M**easurable
- **A**chievable
- **R**elevant
- **T**ime-bound

### Research-Based Limitations

1. **SMART Goals Undervalue Ambition**: The words "achievable" and "realistic" may lead people to define such modest goals that it would be almost impossible not to meet them. This creates a ceiling effect where goals cap performance rather than drive it.

2. **SMART Goals Focus Narrowly on Individual Performance**: They don't encourage transparency or alignment. Goals may not be tied to an overall company or team strategy, and individuals often don't share their goals with others.

3. **SMART Goals Ignore Ongoing Discussion**: The framework implies a "set it and forget it" approach. Once a SMART goal is written, there's no built-in mechanism for frequent review, discussion, or adaptation.

4. **SMART Goals Lack Flexibility**: They may not sufficiently accommodate external changes such as market dynamics or unexpected challenges, leading to rigidity and lacking mid-course reassessment.

5. **SMART Goals Can Promote Gaming**: When goals are achievable by definition, people learn to set easy targets and then declare victory.

## 3.2 FAST Goals (MIT Sloan Research)

### Origin

FAST goals were introduced by Professors Donald Sull and Charles Sull through MIT Sloan Management Review after studying goal-setting practices across hundreds of organizations.

### The FAST Framework

```
F - FREQUENTLY DISCUSSED
  - Goals should be embedded in ongoing conversations
  - Not set once and reviewed annually
  - Regular check-ins, progress updates, course corrections
  - Goals should be a living part of daily/weekly management

A - AMBITIOUS
  - Goals should stretch the team beyond what's comfortable
  - "Realistic" goals often cap performance
  - Ambitious goals activate creativity and effort
  - Failure to fully achieve an ambitious goal may still represent excellent progress
  - Target 60-70% achievement on stretch goals (vs. 100% on safe goals)

S - SPECIFIC
  - Translated into concrete metrics, milestones, and action plans
  - Clear enough that anyone can understand what success looks like
  - Specific does NOT mean modest -- be specific about ambitious targets
  - Include both what will be achieved and how progress will be measured

T - TRANSPARENT
  - Visible to everyone in the organization
  - Teams and individuals can see each other's goals
  - Creates peer accountability and alignment
  - Enables collaboration and prevents duplicated effort
  - Employees who can see their manager's goals are better aligned
```

### SMART vs. FAST Comparison

```
DIMENSION           SMART                    FAST
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Ambition Level      Achievable/Realistic     Ambitious (stretch)
Visibility          Often private            Transparent to all
Discussion Cadence  Set-and-review           Frequently discussed
Flexibility         Fixed once set           Adapted through discussion
Alignment           Individual-focused       Organization-wide
Time Horizon        Time-bound (static)      Ongoing with milestones
Achievement Target  100% expected            60-70% on stretch goals
```

### When to Use FAST vs. SMART

| Use FAST When...                          | Use SMART When...                         |
|-------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Driving organizational transformation     | Individual skill development              |
| Goals require cross-team collaboration    | Compliance or regulatory requirements     |
| Innovation and ambition matter most       | Precise project deliverables              |
| You need cultural change around goals     | Onboarding new employees to goal-setting  |
| Organization is mature enough for stretch | Team is new to structured goals           |

## 3.3 BHAGs (Big Hairy Audacious Goals) -- Jim Collins

### Definition

A BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal, pronounced "bee-hag") is a long-term, 10 to 25-year goal that is so bold it seems almost unreasonable, yet so clear and compelling that it requires no explanation. Coined by Jim Collins and Jerry Porras in "Built to Last."

### Key Characteristics

1. **Clear Finish Line**: You can definitively know when you've achieved it
2. **Tangible and Energizing**: People "get it" right away; little explanation needed
3. **Bold but Not Foolish**: 50-70% chance of achievement (not 10%, not 90%)
4. **Long Time Horizon**: 10-25 years to achieve
5. **Reaches Out and Grabs People**: Emotionally compelling, not just intellectually sound

### Four Types of BHAGs

```
1. TARGET BHAGs (quantitative)
   Example: "Become a $1 billion company by 2035"
   Example: "Serve 1 million customers"

2. COMPETITIVE BHAGs (relative to others)
   Example: "Become #1 or #2 in every market we serve" (GE)
   Example: "Crush Adidas" (Nike in the 1960s)

3. ROLE MODEL BHAGs (emulate a leader)
   Example: "Become the Nike of the consulting industry"
   Example: "Be the Harvard of online education"

4. INTERNAL TRANSFORMATION BHAGs (change who you are)
   Example: "Transform from a product company to a platform company"
   Example: "Become a company where the best people in the world want to work"
```

### Famous BHAG Examples

- **Microsoft (1980s)**: "A computer on every desk and in every home"
- **SpaceX**: "Build a sustainable colony on Mars"
- **Google**: "Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible"
- **Kennedy (1961)**: "Landing a man on the moon and returning him safely to earth before this decade is out"
- **GE (Welch era)**: "Become #1 or #2 in every market we serve and revolutionize this company to have the speed and agility of a small enterprise"

### BHAG Implementation for Small Business

```
BHAG DEVELOPMENT TEMPLATE

1. YOUR 10-25 YEAR BHAG:
   [State the audacious goal in one sentence]

2. TYPE: [Target / Competitive / Role Model / Internal Transformation]

3. WHY THIS IS A TRUE BHAG:
   - Is it clear? Can someone understand it in 10 seconds? [Y/N]
   - Is it bold? Does it feel slightly scary? [Y/N]
   - Is the probability of success 50-70%? [Y/N]
   - Does it energize the team? [Y/N]

4. THE CASCADE:
   BHAG (10-25 years) --> 3-Year Picture --> 1-Year Plan --> Quarterly Rocks --> Weekly Actions

5. HOW EVERY EMPLOYEE CONNECTS:
   "My daily work contributes to the BHAG because..."
   [Every team member should be able to complete this sentence]
```

## 3.4 Which Goal-Setting Framework Works Best for Which Context

### Framework Selection Guide

```
FRAMEWORK       BEST FOR                    TIME HORIZON    TEAM SIZE     COMPLEXITY
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
BHAGs           Vision & direction          10-25 years     Any           Low (one goal)
OKRs            Quarterly execution         90 days         5-500+        Medium
12 Week Year    Individual execution        12 weeks        1-20          Medium
FAST Goals      Org-wide alignment          Varies          50+           High
SMART Goals     Individual task clarity     Days-months     1-5           Low
EOS Rocks       Quarterly priorities        90 days         10-250        Medium
4DX WIGs        Breakthrough results        Varies          Any           Medium

RECOMMENDED STACK FOR SMALL BUSINESS (5-50 employees):

  Layer 1: BHAG (10+ year vision)
```

# SECTION 4: THE CASCADE FRAMEWORK -- CONNECTING DAILY TASKS TO QUARTERLY OUTCOMES

## 4.1 How to Connect a Daily Task to a Quarterly Outcome

### The Connection Chain

Every daily task should be traceable back to a quarterly outcome through a logical chain. If it can't be traced, either the task shouldn't be a priority or a link in the chain is missing.

```
THE TRACEABILITY TEST:

  "I am doing [DAILY TASK] because it directly contributes to [WEEKLY PRIORITY],
   which moves the needle on [MONTHLY MILESTONE/KEY RESULT],
   which achieves [QUARTERLY GOAL/ROCK],
   which advances [ANNUAL OBJECTIVE],
   which builds toward [3-5 YEAR VISION/BHAG]."

EXAMPLE:

  DAILY: I'm making 10 outbound calls to qualified prospects
  BECAUSE --> My weekly priority is to generate 5 discovery meetings
  WHICH MOVES --> My monthly KR of 20 discovery meetings
  WHICH ACHIEVES --> My quarterly goal of $250K in new pipeline
  WHICH ADVANCES --> Our annual objective of $1M revenue growth
  WHICH BUILDS TOWARD --> Our BHAG of becoming the #1 provider in our region
```

### When You Can't Trace a Task

If a daily task can't be connected to the chain:

1. **It might be necessary maintenance** (admin, compliance, operations) -- categorize it as "whirlwind" work
2. **It might be a distraction** -- eliminate or delegate it
3. **A link in the chain might be missing** -- you need to update your quarterly goals
4. **It might signal a strategic gap** -- bring it up in the weekly meeting

```
LEVEL 1: VISION (10-25 Year BHAG)
```

## 4.3 How EOS Rocks Cascade to Weekly To-Dos

### EOS Cascade Structure

The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS) from Gino Wickman's "Traction" uses a clear cascade:

```
EOS VISION/TRACTION ORGANIZER (V/TO):

  10-YEAR TARGET (BHAG)
    "What does success look like in 10 years?"
    Example: "$50M revenue, 200 employees, 3 locations"

  3-YEAR PICTURE
    "What does the company look like in 3 years?"
    Example: "$15M revenue, 50 employees, profitable in all divisions"

  1-YEAR PLAN (Annual Goals)
    "What must we achieve this year?"
    Example: Revenue: $8M, Profit: 15%, NPS: 70+

  QUARTERLY ROCKS (90-Day Priorities)
    "What are the 3-7 most important things for this quarter?"
    Company Rocks owned by leadership team members
    Each leader also has 1-3 individual Rocks

  WEEKLY TO-DOS (7-Day Commitments)
    "What will I get done in the next 7 days?"
    Captured in the Level 10 meeting
    Must be completable in 7 days
    Binary: DONE or NOT DONE (no partial credit)
```

### EOS Level 10 Meeting -- How Rocks Become Weekly To-Dos

```
LEVEL 10 MEETING AGENDA (90 minutes, same day/time weekly)

  1. SEGUE / CHECK-IN (5 min)
     - Share one personal and one business win

  2. SCORECARD REVIEW (5 min)
     - Review 5-15 key metrics
     - Identify any off-track numbers --> add to Issues List

  3. ROCK REVIEW (5 min)
     - Each Rock owner: "On track" or "Off track"
     - Off-track Rocks --> add to Issues List

  4. CUSTOMER/EMPLOYEE HEADLINES (5 min)
     - Good news: celebrate
     - Bad news: add to Issues List

  5. TO-DO LIST REVIEW (5 min)
     - Last week's to-dos: "Done" or "Not done"
     - No discussion, no excuses
     - Target: 90%+ completion rate

  6. IDS -- IDENTIFY, DISCUSS, SOLVE (60 min)
     - Prioritize the Issues List (most important first)
     - For each issue:
       * IDENTIFY: State the real issue (not symptoms)
       * DISCUSS: Everyone contributes (no tangents)
       * SOLVE: Decide on a solution --> creates new To-Dos
     - Work through as many issues as time allows

  7. CONCLUDE (5 min)
     - Recap new to-dos (who does what by when)
     - Determine messages to cascade to teams
     - Rate the meeting 1-10
```

```
  Week 10: To-Do: "Collect feedback from pilot; make adjustments"
  Week 11: To-Do: "Finalize documentation and SOPs"
  Week 12: To-Do: "Full launch; sunset old system"
  Week 13: To-Do: "Review metrics: time-to-onboard, client satisfaction"
```

## 4.4 How 4DX WIGs Cascade to Lead Measures to Daily Commitments

### The 4 Disciplines of Execution (4DX) Framework

From Chris McChesney, Sean Covey, and Jim Huling. 4DX is designed to achieve breakthrough goals in the "midst of the whirlwind" (day-to-day operations).

### Discipline 1: Focus on the Wildly Important Goal (WIG)

```
WIG FORMULA:

  "From X to Y by When"

EXAMPLES:
  "Increase annual revenue from $3M to $4.5M by December 31"
  "Reduce customer churn from 8% to 3% by Q4"
  "Improve employee engagement score from 62 to 85 by year-end"

RULES:
  - Maximum 2 WIGs at any time (1 is ideal)
  - Must be the goal that, if not achieved, makes everything else irrelevant
  - Must have a clear lag measure (the outcome you want)
```

### Discipline 2: Act on Lead Measures

```
LEAD MEASURES vs. LAG MEASURES:

  WIG: "Increase revenue from $3M to $4.5M" (LAG -- you can measure it but can't directly do it)

  LEAD MEASURES (what you CAN directly do):
    Lead Measure 1: "Complete 20 face-to-face sales meetings per week"
    Lead Measure 2: "Send 50 personalized proposals per month"

CHARACTERISTICS OF GOOD LEAD MEASURES:
  1. PREDICTIVE: If we do this, the WIG will move
  2. INFLUENCEABLE: The team can directly control it
  3. MEASURABLE: Can be tracked weekly
  4. SIMPLE: Easy to understand and execute

LEAD MEASURE TEMPLATE:
  "We will [specific action] [frequency] at a rate of [target number]"
```

### Discipline 3: Keep a Compelling Scoreboard

```
WIG SCOREBOARD FORMAT:

  ================================================
  WIG: Increase revenue from $3M to $4.5M
  ================================================

  LAG MEASURE (where we ARE):
  [====================--------] $3.8M / $4.5M (84%)

  LEAD MEASURE 1: Sales Meetings/Week (target: 20)
  Week 1: ||||||||||||||||||| 19
  Week 2: |||||||||||||||||||||||| 24
  Week 3: |||||||||||||||||| 18
  Week 4: ||||||||||||||||||||| 21
  Average: 20.5 (ON TRACK)

  LEAD MEASURE 2: Proposals/Month (target: 50)
  This Month: |||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||| 34/50 (68%)
  (NEED 16 MORE IN REMAINING 8 DAYS)

  ================================================

SCOREBOARD RULES:
  - Must be visible to the entire team (wall-mounted or digital display)
  - Must be updated at least weekly (ideally daily for lead measures)
  - Must be simple enough to understand in 5 seconds
  - Must show both lead AND lag measures
  - Must clearly indicate "winning" vs. "losing"
```

### Discipline 4: Create a Cadence of Accountability

```
WEEKLY WIG SESSION FORMAT (20-30 minutes)

  1. ACCOUNT (5 min)
     "Last week I committed to [X]. Here's what happened..."
     - Report on last week's commitments
     - Did you do what you said you'd do?
     - Binary: YES or NO

  2. REVIEW THE SCOREBOARD (5 min)
     - Are lead measures on track?
     - Is the lag measure moving?
     - Where are we winning? Where are we losing?

  3. PLAN (10-15 min)
     "This week I will..."
     - Each person makes 1-2 specific commitments
     - Commitments must:
       * Start with "I will..."
       * Be completable in 7 days
       * Directly move a lead measure
       * Be specific (not vague)

GOOD COMMITMENT: "I will complete 5 face-to-face meetings with qualified
                   prospects from the Charlotte market this week."

BAD COMMITMENT:  "I'll try to do more sales meetings."
```

### 4DX Cascade Example

```
COMPANY WIG: Increase annual revenue from $3M to $4.5M

  SALES TEAM WIG: Increase new client revenue from $1.2M to $2M
    Lead Measure 1: 20 discovery calls per week
    Lead Measure 2: 10 proposals sent per week

    INDIVIDUAL (Sales Rep A):
      Personal WIG: Close $500K in new business
      Lead Measure: 5 discovery calls per week
      Daily Commitment Example: "Today I will make 3 calls to warm leads
                                 and 2 calls to new prospects from the
                                 Charlotte list."

  MARKETING TEAM WIG: Generate 200 marketing-qualified leads per month
    Lead Measure 1: Publish 3 content pieces per week
    Lead Measure 2: Run 2 targeted ad campaigns per month

    INDIVIDUAL (Content Manager):
      Personal WIG: Produce 36 pieces of content this quarter
      Lead Measure: 3 pieces per week (1 blog, 1 video, 1 social)
      Daily Commitment Example: "Today I will write and publish the blog
                                 post on '5 Signs You Need a Financial Advisor.'"
```

# SECTION 5: THE "LEAD DOMINO" CONCEPT

## 5.1 Identifying the ONE Daily Activity That Moves Everything Else

### The Core Concept

From Gary Keller's "The ONE Thing": A single domino can bring down another domino that is 50% larger. Through geometric progression, a sequence of dominoes that starts with a 2-inch piece can topple a domino the size of the Empire State Building by the 57th domino. The business implication: find the ONE activity that, if done consistently, creates a chain reaction that makes everything else easier or unnecessary.

### The Focusing Question

```
THE FOCUSING QUESTION:

  "What's the ONE thing I can do such that by doing it
   everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"

APPLYING TIME FRAMES:

  For my LIFE:
  "What's the ONE thing I can do in my life right now such that..."

  For my BUSINESS this QUARTER:
  "What's the ONE thing I can do this quarter such that..."

  For my ROLE this WEEK:
  "What's the ONE thing I can do this week such that..."

  For my GOALS TODAY:
  "What's the ONE thing I can do today such that..."

THE NARROWING PROCESS:
  Someday Goal --> 5-Year Goal --> 1-Year Goal --> Monthly Goal -->
  Weekly Goal --> Daily Goal --> RIGHT NOW
```

---

## 5.2 How to Identify Lead Dominoes for Each Business Function

### The Lead Domino Identification Process

```
STEP 1: LIST ALL ACTIVITIES in the function (brain dump)
STEP 2: For each activity, ask: "If I ONLY did this one thing, would results still improve?"
STEP 3: For each activity, ask: "Does this activity make other activities easier or unnecessary?"
STEP 4: The activity with the most YES answers in Step 2 and Step 3 is your lead domino
STEP 5: Validate by testing for 2-4 weeks; measure downstream effects
```

## 6.3 Common Failures in Goal-Setting Systems and How to Prevent Them

### Failure 1: Complexity Overload

```
THE PROBLEM:
  Too many goals, too many metrics, too many meetings.
  Teams spend more time tracking than doing.

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - People complain the system is "too much overhead"
  - Scorecard has 30+ metrics
  - OKR document is 10+ pages
  - Meetings run over time every week

THE FIX:
  - Maximum 3 company goals per quarter
  - Maximum 5-7 metrics per scorecard
  - Start with weekly meeting only; add daily huddle after 1 quarter
  - Ask: "If we could only track 3 things, what would they be?"
```

### Failure 2: No Connection to Daily Work

```
THE PROBLEM:
  Goals exist in a document; daily work happens in email, Slack, and fire-fighting.
  The two worlds never intersect.

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - People can't name the company's quarterly priorities
  - Goals are only discussed in formal quarterly reviews
  - Daily decisions are made without reference to goals
  - "That's a great idea, but it's not in our goals" never gets said

THE FIX:
  - Start every meeting by referencing a specific goal
  - Weekly priorities must explicitly link to a quarterly goal
  - Daily huddle question: "What am I doing today that moves a Rock?"
  - Make goals physically visible (wall poster, digital dashboard)
```

### Failure 3: Goodhart's Law ("Every measure that becomes a target becomes a bad measure")

```
THE PROBLEM:
  When you measure something and attach consequences to it, people optimize
  for the metric rather than the underlying goal.

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - Sales team closes bad-fit deals to hit revenue targets
  - Marketing inflates lead numbers with low-quality contacts
  - Customer service rushes calls to hit "time per call" metrics
  - Employees game the system rather than serve the purpose

THE FIX:
  - Always pair a quantity metric with a quality metric
    (e.g., "Close $500K" paired with "Client NPS 70+")
  - Separate OKRs from compensation (use different metrics for bonuses)
  - Regularly ask: "Is this metric still measuring what we care about?"
  - Review metrics quarterly for gaming/corruption
```

### Failure 4: Leadership Doesn't Model It

```
THE PROBLEM:
  CEO asks teams to set goals but doesn't have personal OKRs.
  Leadership skips weekly meetings when "things are busy."

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - "Do as I say, not as I do" culture
  - Teams stop taking goals seriously
  - Meeting attendance drops
  - Scorecards stop getting updated

THE FIX:
  - CEO must have visible, personal OKRs/Rocks
  - Leadership team never cancels the weekly meeting
  - CEO shares their execution score publicly
  - Leaders go first in every check-in
```

### Failure 5: All Goals, No Execution System

```
THE PROBLEM:
  Great goals are set but there's no system to translate them into daily behavior.
  The gap between "quarterly goal" and "what I do today" is a void.

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - Beautiful goal documents that nobody references
  - Week 1 is energized; Week 4 is back to normal
  - End-of-quarter scramble to show progress
  - Goals are scored but lessons aren't applied

THE FIX:
  - Implement the full cascade: Quarterly --> Weekly --> Daily
  - Use the 12 Week Year weekly scoring system
  - Block time for goal-related work (not just meetings ABOUT goals)
  - The weekly meeting must produce specific to-dos
```

### Failure 6: Never Adjusting

```
THE PROBLEM:
  Goals are set in stone. When circumstances change, the goals don't.
  Teams pursue outdated goals while new realities demand different action.

THE SYMPTOMS:
  - "But that's what we committed to in January"
  - Team achieves goals that no longer matter
  - Obvious opportunities are ignored because they're "not in the plan"
  - Quarterly reviews reveal goals were irrelevant by Week 4

THE FIX:
  - Monthly review includes "Are these still the right goals?"
  - Permission to adjust Key Results mid-quarter (Objectives stay stable)
  - 80/20 rule: 80% committed plan, 20% flexible for emerging priorities
  - "Good judgment" clause: if a goal clearly doesn't make sense, escalate immediately
```

## 6.5 Meta-Metrics: How to Know If Your Measurement System Itself Is Working

### The Measurement System Health Check

You need to measure whether your measurement system is actually working. Review these meta-metrics quarterly:

```
META-METRIC 1: PARTICIPATION RATE
  Question: "What percentage of people are actively using the system?"
  Target: 90%+
  Measured by: Scorecard update rate, meeting attendance, huddle participation
  Red Flag: If less than 70% of people are participating, the system is dying

META-METRIC 2: GOAL AWARENESS
  Question: "Can every employee name the company's top 3 priorities?"
  Target: 90%+ can name them
  Measured by: Random spot-checks or brief quarterly survey
  Red Flag: If less than 50% can name them, communication has broken down

META-METRIC 3: EXECUTION SCORE TREND
  Question: "Are execution scores improving, stable, or declining?"
  Target: Stable at 80-90% after initial ramp-up
  Measured by: 12-week execution score trend
  Red Flag: Declining scores for 3+ consecutive weeks

META-METRIC 4: GOAL ACHIEVEMENT RATE
  Question: "What percentage of quarterly goals are being achieved?"
  Target: 70-80% of goals fully achieved
  Measured by: End-of-quarter scoring
  Red Flag: Below 50% = goals are too ambitious or execution system is broken
  Red Flag: Above 90% = goals aren't ambitious enough

META-METRIC 5: MEETING QUALITY SCORE
  Question: "Do people rate our weekly meetings as valuable?"
  Target: Average rating of 8+ out of 10
  Measured by: End-of-meeting rating (Level 10 style)
  Red Flag: Average below 7 for 4+ consecutive weeks

META-METRIC 6: METRIC RELEVANCE
  Question: "Are we still measuring the right things?"
  Target: 0 metrics on the scorecard that no one looks at or acts on
  Measured by: Quarterly audit -- for each metric, ask "Did we make a decision
               based on this metric in the last 90 days?"
  Red Flag: Any metric that hasn't influenced a decision in 90 days should be removed

META-METRIC 7: TIME-TO-INSIGHT
  Question: "How quickly does a problem show up in our data?"
  Target: Critical issues visible within 1 week
  Measured by: Time between problem occurrence and detection via metrics
  Red Flag: Problems consistently discovered through complaints rather than data

META-METRIC 8: BEHAVIORAL CHANGE
  Question: "Has the measurement system actually changed how people work?"
  Target: Teams reference goals and metrics in everyday conversation
  Measured by: Observation -- do people say things like "That helps our Rock"
               or "That will hurt our close rate metric"
  Red Flag: Goals and metrics are only discussed in formal meetings
```

# SECTION 7: INTEGRATED IMPLEMENTATION GUIDE

## 7.1 The Recommended Stack for a Small Business (5-50 Employees)

```
RECOMMENDED GOAL-SETTING ARCHITECTURE:

LAYER 1: BHAG (10-25 Year Vision)
  - One bold, inspiring long-term goal
  - Communicated constantly
  - Posted on the wall / in every meeting

LAYER 2: 3-YEAR PICTURE (Medium-Term Direction)
  - Revenue target, profit target, team size
  - Key capabilities to build
  - Markets to serve

LAYER 3: 1-YEAR PLAN (Annual Objectives)
  - 3-5 major objectives for the year
  - Revenue, profit, and 2-3 strategic priorities
  - Set during annual planning offsite

LAYER 4: QUARTERLY ROCKS / OKRs (90-Day Priorities)
  - 3-7 company Rocks with individual owners
  - Each Rock has clear success criteria
  - Scored at end of quarter

LAYER 5: WEEKLY PRIORITIES (7-Day Commitments)
  - 3-5 to-dos per person, due in 7 days
  - Must connect to a Rock/OKR
  - Reviewed in weekly meeting (done/not done)

LAYER 6: DAILY LEAD DOMINO (The ONE Thing Today)
  - One critical action per day
  - Time-blocked and protected
  - Tracked on personal scoreboard
```

## 7.2 30-Day Implementation Roadmap

```
WEEK 1: FOUNDATION
  Day 1: Leadership reads this document; agrees on approach
  Day 2: Draft or review BHAG and 3-Year Picture
  Day 3: Set 3-5 quarterly Rocks (with owners and success criteria)
  Day 4: Create CEO scorecard (5-7 key metrics)
  Day 5: Schedule the weekly meeting (same day/time forever)

WEEK 2: TEAM SETUP
  Day 6: Each department head creates their scorecard (5-7 metrics)
  Day 7: Each leader sets 1-3 individual Rocks aligned to company Rocks
  Day 8: Create visible scoreboard (physical or digital)
  Day 9: Train team on weekly meeting format
  Day 10: Hold first weekly meeting (expect it to be imperfect)

WEEK 3: DAILY HABITS
  Day 11: Start daily huddles (10 min, standing, same time)
  Day 12: Each person identifies their lead domino for the week
  Day 13: Introduce daily tracking (15 min morning + 10 min evening)
  Day 14: Each person creates their personal weekly scorecard
  Day 15: Second weekly meeting (should be smoother)

WEEK 4: REFINEMENT
  Day 16: Review what's working and what's not
  Day 17: Adjust scorecards based on first 2 weeks of data
  Day 18: Refine meeting agendas (too long? too short? right topics?)
  Day 19: Celebrate early wins publicly
  Day 20: Commit to the system for the full quarter (minimum viable trial)
```

## 7.3 System Selection Decision Tree

```
WHICH SYSTEM SHOULD I USE?

START HERE: How many employees do you have?

  1-5 employees:
    --> Use: 12 Week Year + Lead Domino + Simple Scorecard
    --> Skip: Formal OKR cascading, EOS (too heavy)
    --> Weekly meeting: 60 minutes
    --> Skip daily huddle until 5+ people

  5-20 employees:
    --> Use: EOS (Rocks + Level 10 + Scorecard) OR OKRs
    --> Add: Daily huddle, 12 Week Year scoring for individual execution
    --> Add: Lead Domino concept for each function leader
    --> Full weekly rhythm

  20-50 employees:
    --> Use: EOS or OKRs with full cascade
    --> Add: 4DX for breakthrough initiatives (beyond the whirlwind)
    --> Add: Department-level scorecards
    --> Full meeting cadence (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly)

  50+ employees:
    --> Use: OKRs with alignment (not strict cascade)
    --> Add: FAST goal principles (transparency, ambition)
    --> Add: 4DX for cross-functional WIGs
    --> Full meeting cadence with department-level meetings
```

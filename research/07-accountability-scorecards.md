## 1.1 EOS Scorecard -- The Weekly Business Pulse

### What It Is

The EOS Scorecard is a weekly dashboard of 5-15 key performance indicators (KPIs) that give you the pulse of your business. Unlike traditional financial statements that look backward, the Scorecard includes leading indicators that help you spot trends and problems before they become serious. It is one of the six core tools in Gino Wickman's Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS), as described in *Traction*.

### Exact Column Layout

The EOS Scorecard is a table with the following precise structure (left to right):

| Column | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Measurable** | The name of the metric -- specific, quantifiable, never subjective |
| **Who** | The single person accountable -- never shared between two people |
| **Goal** | The weekly target or threshold (exact number, min, max, or range) |
| **Weeks 1-13** | 13 columns of weekly data (one full quarter rolling view) |

This creates a **16-column table**: Measurable + Who + Goal + 13 weekly data columns.

### Number of Metrics: The 5-15 Rule

- **Fewer than 5**: Flying blind -- not enough data for a real pulse
- **5 to 15**: The sweet spot -- enough visibility without overwhelming
- **More than 15**: Lost focus -- too many metrics dilute attention
- **Per department**: 3-5 measurables per department or per person

### What Makes a Good Measurable

- **Forward-looking and activity-based**: Numbers that lead to desired outcomes
- **Weekly**: Updated and reviewed every single week without exception
- **Owned by one person**: No shared accountability
- **Quantifiable**: Always a number, never a subjective assessment
- **Actionable**: If off track, the owner can take specific steps to correct it

### The 13-Week Trailing View

- 13 weeks = one quarter, providing a full-quarter perspective
- Prevents overreacting to a single off week
- Patterns become visible -- declining, spiking, or stable trends
- After 14 weeks of data, the oldest week rolls off but is stored for historical analysis

### Weekly Review Process (in the Level 10 Meeting)

The Scorecard review takes exactly **5 minutes**:
1. Facilitator goes through each measurable
2. Each owner reports: **"On Track"** or **"Off Track"**
3. No explanations during the review -- just the binary status
4. Off-track numbers get "dropped" to the Issues List for IDS discussion later
5. The 5-minute constraint enforces discipline

## 1.2 Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton) -- Simplified for SMBs

### The Four Perspectives

The Balanced Scorecard (BSC), introduced by Robert S. Kaplan and David P. Norton in 1992, integrates financial and non-financial performance measures across four perspectives:

```
        FINANCIAL (Outcomes)
          "How do we look to owners?"
             ^
             |
        CUSTOMER (Value Delivered)
          "How do customers see us?"
             ^
             |
        INTERNAL PROCESS (How We Deliver)
          "What must we excel at?"
             ^
             |
        LEARNING & GROWTH (Foundation)
          "Can we continue to improve?"
```

**The cause-and-effect chain**: Invest in employee skills and systems (Learning & Growth) --> enables better internal processes (Internal Process) --> creates more value for customers (Customer) --> drives financial results (Financial).

### Simplified BSC for Small Business (2-3 Objectives Per Perspective)

**FINANCIAL PERSPECTIVE:**
| Objective | KPI | Target |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Grow Revenue | Revenue Growth Rate (%) | 15-25% YoY |
| Improve Profitability | Net Profit Margin (%) | 10-25% |
| Strengthen Cash | Operating Cash Flow ($) | Consistently positive |

**CUSTOMER PERSPECTIVE:**
| Objective | KPI | Target |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Increase Loyalty | Customer Retention Rate (%) | 80-95% |
| Acquire Efficiently | Customer Acquisition Cost ($) | <1/3 of LTV |
| Deliver Value | Customer Satisfaction Score | 4.5+/5.0 |

**INTERNAL PROCESS PERSPECTIVE:**
| Objective | KPI | Target |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Deliver On Time | On-Time Delivery Rate (%) | 95%+ |
| Maintain Quality | Defect/Error Rate (%) | <2% |
| Improve Efficiency | Process Automation Rate (%) | Increasing QoQ |

**LEARNING & GROWTH PERSPECTIVE:**
| Objective | KPI | Target |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Build Capability | Training Hours per Employee | 40+ hours/year |
| Engage & Retain | Employee Net Promoter Score | 30+ |
| Invest in Systems | Technology Adoption Rate (%) | >80% |

### BSC vs. EOS: How They Complement Each Other

| Dimension | EOS Scorecard | Balanced Scorecard |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Purpose** | Operational pulse -- are we executing? | Strategic alignment -- are we moving toward vision? |
| **Cadence** | Weekly | Monthly/Quarterly |
| **Scope** | 13-week rolling | Annual |
| **KPI Count** | 5-15 company, 3-5 per dept | 2-3 per perspective (8-12 total) |
| **Best For** | Weekly execution tracking | Strategic direction setting |

## 1.3 The "Vital Few" Principle -- How Many KPIs Are Optimal?

### George A. Miller's Research: "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" (1956)

People can only hold **7 +/- 2 items** (5-9) in short-term memory at any one time. If you present more than ~9 metrics on a dashboard, the brain's ability to process, remember, and act on them degrades rapidly.

### Joseph Juran & the Pareto Principle

Roughly **20% of metrics capture 80% of actionable business intelligence**. The "vital few" metrics provide the vast majority of insight needed to manage the business.

### Research-Based Recommendations by Level

| Level | Recommended KPIs | Rationale |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| **CEO / Executive** | 5-7 | Personal dashboard checked daily/weekly |
| **Per Strategic Goal** | 2-3 per goal | ClearPoint: "9-11 strategic measures total" |
| **Per Person / Role** | 3-5 maximum | EOS: each person owns at least 1, typically 1-3 |
| **Per Department** | 5-8 | Start with 5 and build from there |
| **Total Organization** | 15-25 strategic | Separate operational from strategic metrics |

### The "5-5-5 Rule" (Synthesized Framework)

Based on converging research from multiple sources:
- **5 company-level KPIs** reviewed weekly by leadership
- **5 KPIs per department** reviewed weekly by department head
- **5 or fewer KPIs per individual** reviewed daily/weekly

### The Cost of Too Many KPIs

The diminishing returns curve is steep:
- Going from 0 to 5 KPIs provides enormous value
- Going from 15 to 25 provides minimal additional value and significant additional cost
- **Track too many KPIs and you spend all your time collecting data and none acting on it**

## 1.4 Leading vs. Lagging Indicators -- Complete Framework

### Core Definitions

| Characteristic | Leading Indicator | Lagging Indicator |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Time orientation** | Predictive / forward-looking | Retrospective / backward-looking |
| **Controllability** | Directly influenceable through daily actions | Result of accumulated past actions |
| **Speed of change** | Changes quickly when effort changes | Changes slowly, with a delay |
| **Use case** | Course correction, coaching, daily management | Strategy validation, goal tracking |
| **Question answered** | "Are we doing the right activities?" | "Did we achieve the desired result?" |
| **Example timing** | Can be measured daily/weekly | Often measured monthly/quarterly |

### Critical Principle

**Every lagging indicator should have at least one paired leading indicator.** If you only track lagging indicators, you are driving by looking in the rearview mirror.

### The Recommended Balance

Daily/weekly tracking should be **60-70% leading indicators**. Monthly/quarterly reviews should include both.

### Paired Examples by Business Function

**SALES:**
| Leading (What You DO) | Lagging (What You GET) | If-Then Logic |
|------------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| Outbound calls/day | Monthly revenue | More calls --> more conversations --> more closes |
| Proposals delivered/week | Quarterly closed deals | More proposals --> more wins |
| Pipeline coverage ratio (3x target) | Win rate % | Sufficient pipeline sustains healthy close rate |
| Lead response time | Customer acquisition cost | Faster response --> higher conversion --> lower CAC |

**OPERATIONS:**
| Leading | Lagging | If-Then Logic |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Preventive maintenance completed on schedule | Equipment downtime hours | Proactive maintenance --> fewer breakdowns |
| Process checklist completion rate | Defect/error rate | Procedures followed --> fewer mistakes |
| Safety training completion rate | Workplace incident rate | Training completed --> fewer incidents |

**FINANCE:**
| Leading | Lagging | If-Then Logic |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Weekly cash receipts vs. forecast | Monthly cash position | Weekly tracking --> predictable month-end |
| Invoices sent within 24 hours | Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) | Faster invoicing --> faster payment |
| Budget variance (weekly check) | Quarterly profit margin | Catching overruns early --> protected profit |

**MARKETING:**
| Leading | Lagging | If-Then Logic |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Website traffic / unique visitors | Marketing-qualified leads | More traffic --> more leads |
| Content pieces published/week | Organic search ranking | Consistent content --> improved rankings |
| Email open/click rates | Conversion rate | Higher engagement --> more conversions |

**TEAM / HR:**
| Leading | Lagging | If-Then Logic |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| 1-on-1 meeting completion rate | Employee engagement (eNPS) | Regular check-ins --> sustained engagement |
| Training hours completed | Employee productivity | More training --> better performance |
| Absenteeism rate (early warning) | Annual turnover rate | Rising absence --> future turnover |

## 1.5 Unified Three-Level Architecture

### How EOS + BSC + Vital Few Work Together

| Framework | Purpose | Cadence | KPI Count |
|-----------|---------|---------|-----------|
| **EOS Scorecard** | Operational pulse | Weekly | 5-15 company, 3-5 per dept |
| **Balanced Scorecard** | Strategic alignment | Monthly/Quarterly | 2-3 per perspective (8-12 total) |
| **Vital Few** | Cognitive design | Design principle | 5-7 per person, 5-9 per view |

### Three-Level Scorecard Architecture

**Level 1: CEO/Leadership Weekly Scorecard (EOS Format)**
- 5-8 company-level KPIs
- Reviewed weekly in Level 10 Meeting (5 minutes)
- 13-week rolling view
- Mix of leading and lagging indicators covering all four BSC perspectives

**Level 2: Department Scorecards (BSC-Aligned)**
- 3-5 KPIs per department
- Each KPI maps to one of the four BSC perspectives
- Mix of leading (2-3) and lagging (1-2) indicators
- Feeds up to the company Scorecard

**Level 3: Individual KPIs**
- 1-3 KPIs per person
- Directly tied to their accountability
- Always includes at least one leading indicator the individual directly controls

### Master Design Principles Checklist

1. **Vital Few**: 5-7 KPIs at top level, 3-5 per role (respecting Miller's 7 +/- 2)
2. **Always pair**: Every lagging indicator with at least one leading indicator
3. **One owner per metric**: No shared accountability
4. **13-week view**: Spot trends, not single-week anomalies
5. **Activity-based leading indicators**: Track what people DO, not just what happens
6. **Four-perspective balance**: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth
7. **Cause-and-effect thinking**: Each metric should logically connect to an outcome
8. **Review cadence discipline**: Activity daily, performance weekly, strategic monthly/quarterly
9. **Two-word status**: "On Track" or "Off Track" -- detailed discussion happens separately
10. **Cascade structure**: Company --> department --> individual (everyone's numbers connect)
11. **Action pathway**: Every off-track metric triggers a specific response process
12. **Ruthless pruning**: If nobody makes decisions based on a metric, remove it

## 2.1 Why People Abandon Tracking Systems

Research from *Computers in Human Behavior* (2019) found that **approximately one-third of users abandon tracking systems within a few months**. Primary reasons:

1. **Demotivation** -- the #1 reason. When people stop seeing value, they stop entering data
2. **Interruption or deprioritization** -- competing priorities push tracking off the routine
3. **Habit formation failure** -- if not reinforced in the first 30-60 days, it never becomes automatic
4. **Perceived lack of consequence** -- if nobody reviews the data, entering it feels pointless
5. **Friction in data entry** -- more steps/calculations = faster abandonment

### The Accountability Effect (ASTD Research)

| Accountability Level | Probability of Completion |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Having an idea or goal | 10% |
| Consciously deciding to do it | 25% |
| Deciding when to do it | 40% |
| Planning how to do it | 50% |
| Committing to someone else | 65% |
| Having a specific accountability appointment | **95%** |

**Key insight**: The 95% rate comes from **specific, ongoing accountability appointments** (scheduled check-in meetings), not just having a partner. This is why the weekly scorecard review meeting is more important than the tracking tool itself.

---

## 2.2 The "1-Second Update" Principle

### EOS Scorecard Design Philosophy

The ideal metric requires **no calculation at the point of entry** -- it is a raw count or a yes/no that the person already knows from doing their work:

- Data entry happens on a designated day (typically Friday or Monday)
- Each metric owner simply states "hit" or "miss" -- no lengthy explanations
- The system should be tested and adjusted weekly for accuracy
- **Why weekly (not daily) for the EOS Scorecard**: Daily is too frequent for leadership review -- natural variations cause unnecessary panic. Weekly strikes the balance between trend-spotting and noise filtering

However, underlying **activity data** (calls, emails, units shipped) IS tracked daily by individuals. The weekly scorecard aggregates that daily data.

## 2.3 Paper vs. Digital Tracking -- What Research Says

### Cognitive Science Findings

- **Handwriting on physical paper** leads to greater brain activity and memory retention compared to typing or using a stylus on a tablet (*Frontiers in Psychology*)
- Students writing by hand showed **higher electrical activity across brain regions** for movement, vision, sensory processing, and memory
- **However**, preference alignment matters more than medium: people using their **preferred method** were **3.1x more likely** to maintain consistent tracking

### Completion Rate Comparison

| Dimension | Paper | Digital |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| 30-day completion rate | 59% | 67% |
| Memory encoding | Stronger (motor engagement) | Weaker (automated typing) |
| Automated reminders | None | Push notifications |
| Data visualization | Manual | Automatic charts/trends |
| Scalability | Breaks down beyond 5-7 metrics | Handles unlimited |
| Shareability | Physical presence required | Real-time team sharing |

### The Key Takeaway

**Tracking itself increases success rates by 25-40%** compared to not tracking at all. The specific medium matters far less than: (1) consistency, (2) preference alignment, and (3) external accountability through regular review meetings.

### Recommended Hybrid Approach

1. **Daily data entry**: Digital (phone app or simple web form) for speed and reminders
2. **Weekly team review**: Physical whiteboard or printed scorecard for visual impact and collective accountability
3. **Monthly/quarterly analysis**: Digital dashboard with trend lines and RAG status

## 2.4 The Daily Standup / Daily Huddle

### Verne Harnish / Scaling Up Daily Huddle Format

**Duration:** Strictly 5-15 minutes (optimal: 10-12 min)
**Format:** Standing. No chairs. No laptops for email.
**Frequency:** Every business day, same time, same location

**Three Segments (5 minutes each):**

| Segment | Question | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **What's Up** | "What is happening in the next 24 hours?" | Alignment -- everyone knows each other's focus |
| **Daily Metrics** | "What are today's key numbers?" | Real-time visibility into business health |
| **Stucks** | "Where are you stuck?" | Surface blockers for offline resolution |

### Key Implementation Rules

- **1-2 minutes per person maximum**
- **No problem-solving** -- the meeting identifies issues; solving happens afterward
- **Start on time** even if people are missing
- **Cascading huddles** in larger organizations: executive team first (8:45 AM), then department heads with their teams (9:15 AM), then team leads with direct reports (9:45 AM)

### Recommended Daily Scorecard Display

| Metric | Owner | Daily Goal | Today's Actual | Status |
|--------|-------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Revenue collected | CFO | $5,000 | $4,800 | Yellow |
| New leads | Marketing | 10 | 12 | Green |
| Outbound calls | Sales | 50 | 52 | Green |
| Orders shipped | Ops | 25 | 22 | Yellow |
| Support tickets closed | CS | 15 | 18 | Green |

## 2.5 Traffic Light (RAG) Scoring System

### Color Definitions

| Color | Meaning | Rule of Thumb |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **GREEN** | On track | A good person, doing a good job, achieves green 90% of the time |
| **YELLOW** | At risk; needs monitoring | Between red and green; should be trending in the right direction |
| **RED** | Off track; immediate attention required | Significant issues requiring intervention |

### How to Set Specific Thresholds

**Method 1: Percentage Deviation from Target**

| Predictability & Criticality | Green | Yellow | Red |
|-----------------------------|-------|--------|-----|
| High predictability (daily shipping) | Within 5% | 5-10% off | >10% off |
| Moderate predictability (sales conversion) | Within 10% | 10-20% off | >20% off |
| Low predictability / new activity | Within 15% | 15-25% off | >25% off |

**Method 2: Absolute Threshold Ranges**

| KPI | Green | Yellow | Red |
|-----|-------|--------|-----|
| Budget variance | Within 5% | 5-10% over | >10% over |
| Customer satisfaction | >= 95% | 80-94% | < 80% |
| On-time delivery | >= 95% | 85-94% | < 85% |
| eNPS | >= 30 | 10-29 | < 10 |
| Revenue vs. target | >= 95% | 80-94% | < 80% |
| Defect/error rate | < 2% | 2-5% | > 5% |

### Preventing "Watermelon Reporting" (Green Outside, Red Inside)

| Root Cause | Prevention |
|-----------|------------|
| **Blame culture** | Leaders must reward early Red reporting: "Thank you. What do you need?" |
| **Single-color summarization** | Report RAG per dimension (budget, schedule, scope, quality, risk) |
| **No defined thresholds** | Define measurable thresholds BEFORE the work begins; data determines color |
| **No independent verification** | Periodic spot-checks comparing reported status against actual data |
| **Infrequent review** | Weekly RAG updates with trend indicators (arrows: up/flat/down) |

### Key Tactics

- **"What would make this Red?"** -- Ask this even when status is Green
- **Pair RAG with trend indicator** (arrow up/flat/down) AND a 13-week rolling view
- **Require "basis of assessment" statement** -- 1-2 sentences explaining why the color was assigned
- **Include confidence level** (1-10 scale): "Green, but confidence of 4/10 it stays Green"

## 2.6 Optimal Review Cadences

### The Four-Tier Operating Cadence

```
             /\
            /  \      QUARTERLY: 3-5 strategic KPIs
           / Q  \     (CAC, LTV:CAC, Revenue Growth, Churn)
          /------\
         / MONTH  \    MONTHLY: 5-7 performance KPIs
        /   LY     \   (Margins, NPS, Pipeline Coverage, Budget)
       /------------\
      /   WEEKLY     \   WEEKLY: 5-15 operational KPIs
     /                \  (Win Rate, Leads, Delivery, Cash, Rocks)
    /------------------\
   /      DAILY         \  DAILY: 3-5 activity metrics
  /                      \ (Calls, Emails, Units, Tickets, Revenue)
 /________________________\
```

### Cadence Selection Guidelines

| If you can change the outcome... | Then review... |
|----------------------------------|----------------|
| Within 24 hours | Track and review **daily** |
| Within a week | Track daily, review **weekly** |
| Within a month | Review **monthly** |
| Slowly (strategic) | Review **quarterly** |

**Leading indicators** (activity counts, pipeline, MQLs) should be tracked daily/weekly because you can change them through immediate action. **Lagging indicators** (revenue, margin, churn, NPS) should be reviewed weekly/monthly because daily fluctuations are noise.

**Key principle**: "Those who pulse faster, grow faster" -- Verne Harnish

# 4. Accountability Meeting Structures

## 4.1 The EOS Level 10 Meeting

### The Exact 90-Minute Agenda

| Segment | Duration | What Happens |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **1. Segue** | 5 min | Each person shares one personal best + one professional best |
| **2. Scorecard Review** | 5 min | 5-15 metrics: "On Track" or "Off Track" only. Off-track --> Issues List |
| **3. Rock Review** | 5 min | 3-5 quarterly priorities per person: "On Track" or "Off Track" |
| **4. Headlines** | 5 min | Customer/employee news, good or bad. Noteworthy items --> Issues List |
| **5. To-Do List** | 5 min | 7-day action items: "Done" or "Not Done." No explanations. Target: 90%+ |
| **6. IDS** | 60 min | **I**dentify root cause, **D**iscuss (all input), **S**olve (assign to-dos). Top 3 issues |
| **7. Conclude** | 5 min | Recap to-dos, cascading messages, rate meeting 1-10 (target: 8+) |

### IDS Process Detail

1. **Identify**: "Is this the real issue, or a symptom?" Dig to root cause
2. **Discuss**: Each member provides factual input. Facilitator prevents tangents
3. **Solve**: Agree on solution --> becomes 7-day to-dos. Issue struck permanently

### Who Attends & Facilitates

- **Attendees**: Leadership team (5-8 people). All functional areas represented
- **Facilitator**: Usually the Integrator/COO. Ruthless about time management
- **Scribe**: Captures to-dos, issues, decisions in real time
- **Departmental L10s**: Every team runs its own L10. Issues that can't be resolved escalate up

### Why It Works / Why Teams Abandon It

**Works because:**
- Rigid structure eliminates wasted time
- IDS forces root-cause problem solving
- 7-day to-do cycle creates weekly personal accountability
- Binary reporting eliminates spin and storytelling
- Rating the meeting creates self-correcting pressure

**Teams abandon it when:**
- Someone breaks the structure ("Can we just quickly discuss this?")
- Leaders avoid honesty about "off track" status
- They try to solve every issue instead of prioritizing top 3
- Meetings run over 90 minutes
- The weekly cadence is broken for "busy" periods

## 4.2 The Daily Huddle (Scaling Up / Rockefeller Habits)

### Format

**Duration:** 10-15 minutes maximum. Timer enforced.
**Standing.** No chairs. No problem-solving.
**Same time every day.** Starts even if people are missing.

### The 3 Questions

| # | Question | Time | Purpose |
|---|----------|------|---------|
| 1 | "What's up in the next 24 hours?" | ~5 min | Alignment on today's priorities |
| 2 | "What are the daily metrics?" | ~5 min | Real-time business health visibility |
| 3 | "Where are you stuck?" | ~5 min | Surface blockers for offline resolution |

### Cascading Huddles in Larger Organizations

- Executive team: 8:45 AM
- Department heads with their teams: 9:15 AM
- Team leads with direct reports: 9:45 AM

Information flows top-to-bottom within 60-90 minutes. Blockers flow bottom-to-top through the same cascade.

### How It Connects to the Weekly Meeting

- The weekly meeting replaces one daily huddle (no separate huddle on that day)
- Issues surfaced in daily huddles but not resolved become weekly meeting Issues List items
- Daily metrics aggregate into the weekly Scorecard

## 4.3 The 4DX WIG Session

### The Four Disciplines of Execution (Context)

1. **Focus on the Wildly Important Goal (WIG)**: 1-2 goals maximum. Format: "From X to Y by when"
2. **Act on Lead Measures**: Predictive, influenceable behaviors that drive the WIG
3. **Keep a Compelling Scoreboard**: Simple enough to determine winning/losing in 5 seconds
4. **Create a Cadence of Accountability**: Weekly WIG Sessions

### WIG Session Format (20-30 minutes, weekly)

| Part | Duration | What Happens |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **1. ACCOUNT** | 5 min | Report on last week's personal commitments. Binary: kept or not kept. No partial credit |
| **2. REVIEW** | 5 min | Update and review scoreboard. "Are we winning or losing?" Check lead and lag measures |
| **3. PLAN** | 10-15 min | Each person makes 1-2 specific commitments for this week that directly impact lead measures |

### The Commitment Cycle

1. Make a commitment (Plan phase)
2. Execute the commitment (during the week)
3. Report on the commitment (Account phase next week)
4. Learn from the result (Review phase)
5. Repeat

### Scoreboard Design Requirements

- **WIG** at the top: "From X to Y by when"
- **Lag measure**: Trend line with target line overlaid (visual gap = ahead/behind)
- **Lead measures**: Weekly bar charts or green/red indicators per team member
- **Updated weekly** by designated scorekeeper
- **Physically visible**: Posted in workspace -- not buried in a spreadsheet
- **5-second test**: A visitor must know if the team is winning or losing in 5 seconds

## 4.4 Monthly Business Review (MBR)

### Complete 60-Minute Agenda

| Section | Duration | Presenter | Content |
|---------|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Opening + Last Month's Action Items** | 5 min | CEO/COO | Review previous MBR action items: done/not done |
| **Financial Review** | 15 min | CFO | P&L vs. budget (focus only on >10% variances), revenue by segment, cash flow, AR aging |
| **Operational Review** | 15 min | Dept heads | Key operational KPIs with MoM and YoY trends, quality metrics, customer sat, employee metrics |
| **Sales & Pipeline Review** | 10 min | Sales lead | Pipeline by stage, win/loss analysis, CAC trends, major deals status |
| **Strategic Initiatives Check** | 10 min | Initiative owners | RAG status on Rocks/OKRs, milestones vs. plan, blockers requiring leadership decision |
| **Action Items & Close** | 5 min | CEO/COO | Summarize new action items with owners and deadlines |

### Key MBR Rules

1. **Focus on variances, not every line item** (only discuss >10% or dollar-meaningful)
2. **Always start by reviewing last month's action items** (creates accountability loop)
3. **Assign a timekeeper** who is not the meeting leader
4. **Every action item = one owner + specific deadline** (never a team or committee)
5. **Distribute reports 24 hours before** (meeting time = discussion, not data processing)
6. **Do not solve problems in the MBR** -- identify and assign owners to solve separately

## 4.6 Meeting Length vs. Effectiveness Research

### Attention Span Data

| Timeframe | Attention Level |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 0-15 min | Peak attention and engagement |
| 15-30 min | Begins to decline; first inflection point |
| 30 min | Drops by ~52% compared to start |
| 45 min | Significant concentration loss |
| 60+ min | Mental exhaustion; decisions measurably worse |

### Microsoft EEG Study Findings

- **Without breaks**: Stress (beta waves) rose steadily across consecutive meetings
- **With 10-minute breaks**: Stress remained stable; engagement increased
- **Back-to-back transitions**: Cause neurological stress spikes
- **Recommendation**: Default to 25 or 50 minutes instead of 30 or 60

### Optimal Meeting Lengths by Purpose

| Purpose | Optimal Length | Format |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| Daily standup/huddle | 10-15 min | Standing, info transfer only |
| Status check-in | 15-25 min | Binary reporting |
| Problem-solving/IDS | 45-60 min | Deep discussion; break at 45 min |
| Strategic planning | 90 min max/session | Break at 45 min; multiple sessions > marathon |
| Monthly business review | 60-90 min | Pre-reads distributed 24 hours prior |

**Key insight**: Groups under moderate time pressure perform more optimally than groups with unlimited time. The constraint creates the discipline.

### Comparative Meeting Summary

| Need | Format | Cadence | Duration | Core Mechanism |
|------|--------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Daily alignment | Daily Huddle | Daily | 10-15 min | 3 questions |
| Weekly execution | EOS L10 | Weekly | 90 min | IDS + 7-day to-dos |
| Weekly goal focus | 4DX WIG Session | Weekly | 20-30 min | Commitment cycle |
| Monthly performance | MBR | Monthly | 60-90 min | Financial + operational + strategic review |
| Initiative health | RAG Update | Weekly | 15-25 min | Multi-dimensional Red/Yellow/Green |

# 5. Goal-Setting Systems That Drive Daily Action

## 5.1 OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)

### The Format

```
OBJECTIVE: [Qualitative, inspirational -- WHAT you want to achieve]
  KR1: [Increase/Decrease] [metric] from [X] to [Y] by [date]
  KR2: [Achieve/Complete] [specific deliverable] by [date]
  KR3: [Reach] [target number] of [measurable thing] by [date]

INITIATIVES (the work you do to move Key Results):
  - Initiative A, Initiative B, Initiative C
```

### OKR Scoring (Google's 0.0-1.0 Scale)

| Score | Status | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 0.0-0.3 | RED | Failed to make significant progress |
| 0.4-0.6 | YELLOW | Made progress but fell short |
| 0.7-1.0 | GREEN | Delivered |

**For aspirational OKRs (stretch goals):** 0.6-0.7 = the "sweet spot" of success. Consistently >0.7 means goals aren't ambitious enough.
**For committed OKRs (must-achieve):** 1.0 = expected. Below 1.0 requires root-cause analysis.

### Cascading OKRs: Company --> Department --> Individual

```
LEVEL 1: COMPANY OKRs (CEO/Leadership)
  Objective: Become the market leader in our region
    KR1: Grow revenue from $2M to $3M
    KR2: Achieve NPS of 70+
    KR3: Expand to 3 new geographic markets
         |
         v
LEVEL 2: DEPARTMENT OKRs (aligned to Company KRs)
  SALES (aligned to KR1: Revenue)
    Objective: Build a high-performing sales engine
      KR1: Close $750K in new business per quarter
      KR2: Increase average deal size from $15K to $22K
         |
         v
LEVEL 3: INDIVIDUAL OKRs (aligned to Department KRs)
  SALES REP (aligned to Dept KR1)
    Objective: Consistently exceed my sales targets
      KR1: Close $190K this quarter
      KR2: Maintain 30%+ close rate
```

**Recommendation for small businesses**: Use **alignment** rather than strict cascading. Teams propose their own OKRs, then verify alignment with leadership.

### Weekly OKR Check-In (15-30 min)

1. **Wins & Highlights** (5 min): One win per person
2. **OKR Progress** (10 min): Current score, confidence (RAG), what moved the needle, what's blocking
3. **Priorities This Week** (10 min): Top 1-3 priorities connected to a Key Result
4. **Blockers & Requests** (5 min): Surface obstacles, assign owners

### Common OKR Mistakes

| Mistake | Bad Example | Good Example |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Activities as KRs | "Launch email campaign" | "Increase email conversion from 2% to 5%" |
| Too many OKRs | 5 objectives x 5 KRs = 25 items | 2-3 objectives x 3 KRs = 6-9 items |
| Sandbagging | "Grow revenue 2%" (happening organically) | "Grow revenue 25%" (stretch) |
| Tied to compensation | "Hit OKRs or lose bonus" | OKRs for alignment; separate KPIs for comp |
| Set-and-forget | Set in January, check in March | Weekly check-ins, mid-quarter adjustment |

## 5.2 The "12 Week Year" (Brian Moran)

### Why Annual Goals Fail

1. **End-of-year urgency**: Coast 9 months, scramble in Q4
2. **False sense of time**: "I have 12 months" creates complacency
3. **Delayed feedback**: Don't know you're off track until too late
4. **Lack of urgency**: Every day feels low-stakes with 365 of them

### Why 12 Weeks Works

- **Urgency is constant**: Every week = equivalent of a month in an annual cycle
- **Feedback is fast**: Know by Week 4 if on track
- **Focus intensifies**: Only fit most important goals into 12 weeks
- **4 fresh starts per year**: 4 chances to adjust

### The Weekly Execution Score (The Most Important Metric)

```
Execution Score = (Tactics Completed / Tactics Planned) x 100

BENCHMARKS:
  90-100% = Exceptional (verify goals are ambitious enough)
  85-89%  = SUPERB (Moran's target; nearly guarantees goal achievement)
  70-84%  = Good but needs improvement
  Below 70% = Execution breakdown; requires immediate correction
```

### 12-Week Cycle Calendar

```
Cycle 1: Weeks 1-12 (Jan-Mar)    --> 13th Week: Review/Planning
Cycle 2: Weeks 1-12 (Apr-Jun)    --> 13th Week: Review/Planning
Cycle 3: Weeks 1-12 (Jul-Sep)    --> 13th Week: Review/Planning
Cycle 4: Weeks 1-12 (Oct-Dec)    --> 13th Week: Review/Planning
```

### The 13th Week (Buffer/Transition)

- **Days 1-2**: Score completed cycle, celebrate wins, document lessons
- **Day 3**: Strategic review -- are we still on track to the annual vision?
- **Days 4-5**: Plan next 12-week cycle (set 1-3 goals, define weekly tactics)

### Performance Time Blocking

| Block Type | Duration | Purpose |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Strategic Block** | 3 hours/week min | Uninterrupted work ON the business. No email/phone |
| **Buffer Block** | 30-60 min, 1-2x daily | Handle admin, email, voicemail (batch low-value tasks) |
| **Breakout Block** | 3 hours/week min | Rest, recreation, renewal. Mandatory (prevents burnout) |

## 5.3 SMART Goals Criticism & FAST Goals Alternative

### SMART Goals Limitations (Research-Based)

1. **Undervalue ambition**: "Achievable" and "realistic" cap performance with easy targets
2. **Individual focus**: No built-in transparency or organizational alignment
3. **Set-and-forget**: No mechanism for frequent review or adaptation
4. **Inflexible**: Don't accommodate market changes; lack mid-course reassessment
5. **Promote gaming**: When achievable by definition, people set easy targets

### FAST Goals (MIT Sloan Research)

| Letter | Principle | Contrast with SMART |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **F** | **Frequently discussed** | Not set-and-forget; embedded in ongoing conversations |
| **A** | **Ambitious** | Not "achievable/realistic"; stretch goals that activate effort |
| **S** | **Specific** | Same as SMART -- concrete metrics and milestones |
| **T** | **Transparent** | Visible to everyone; creates peer accountability and alignment |

### Framework Selection Guide

| Framework | Best For | Time Horizon | Team Size |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **BHAGs** | Vision & direction | 10-25 years | Any |
| **OKRs** | Quarterly execution | 90 days | 5-500+ |
| **12 Week Year** | Individual execution | 12 weeks | 1-20 |
| **FAST Goals** | Org-wide alignment | Varies | 50+ |
| **SMART Goals** | Individual task clarity | Days-months | 1-5 |
| **EOS Rocks** | Quarterly priorities | 90 days | 10-250 |
| **4DX WIGs** | Breakthrough results | Varies | Any |

## 5.4 BHAGs (Big Hairy Audacious Goals)

### Key Characteristics

- **Clear finish line**: Definitively know when achieved
- **Tangible and energizing**: People "get it" immediately
- **Bold but not foolish**: 50-70% chance of achievement
- **Long time horizon**: 10-25 years
- **Emotionally compelling**: Grabs people viscerally

### Four Types

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Target** (quantitative) | "Become a $1 billion company by 2035" |
| **Competitive** (relative) | "Crush Adidas" (Nike, 1960s) |
| **Role Model** (emulate) | "Become the Nike of consulting" |
| **Internal Transformation** | "Transform from product company to platform" |

## 5.5 The Cascade Framework -- Connecting Daily Tasks to Quarterly Outcomes

### The Five-Level Strategy Cascade

```
LEVEL 1: VISION (10-25 Year BHAG)
  "Where are we going?"
         |
LEVEL 2: STRATEGY (3-5 Year Strategic Priorities)
  "How will we get there?" (3-5 strategic pillars)
         |
LEVEL 3: QUARTERLY GOALS (90-Day Rocks / OKRs)
  "What must we accomplish this quarter?"
         |
LEVEL 4: WEEKLY PRIORITIES (7-Day Focus Areas)
  "What must get done this week?"
         |
LEVEL 5: DAILY ACTIONS (Today's Non-Negotiable)
  "What is the ONE thing I must do today?"
```

### The Traceability Test

Every daily task should pass this test:

> "I am doing [DAILY TASK] because it contributes to [WEEKLY PRIORITY], which moves [KEY RESULT], which achieves [QUARTERLY GOAL], which advances [ANNUAL OBJECTIVE], which builds toward [BHAG]."

If a task cannot be traced: either it's maintenance/"whirlwind" work, it's a distraction to eliminate, or a link in the chain is missing.

## 5.6 The "Lead Domino" Concept

From Gary Keller's "The ONE Thing": A single domino can topple another 50% larger. By the 57th domino in sequence, one 2-inch piece topples a domino the size of the Empire State Building.

### The Focusing Question

> "What's the ONE thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"

### Lead Dominoes by Function

| Function | The Lead Domino | Daily Practice | Why Everything Else Gets Easier |
|----------|----------------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| **Sales** | Make X qualified outbound contacts/day | Block 90 min each morning for outbound | Pipeline fills, forecasting improves, revenue becomes predictable |
| **Marketing** | Publish X pieces of valuable content/week | 60-90 min daily creating/editing | SEO improves, social has material, email has substance, brand grows |
| **Operations** | Document/improve one process per week | 30 min daily reviewing one process | Training is faster, quality is consistent, bottlenecks surface |
| **Finance** | Review cash flow daily | 15-min morning cash review | Spending decisions informed, collections timely, surprises eliminated |
| **CEO** | Spend first 90 min on #1 strategic priority | 7:00-8:30 AM strategic block, no exceptions | Vision clarifies, strategy executes, team sees what matters |

## 6.4 Common Failures and How to Prevent Them

| Failure | Symptoms | Fix |
|---------|----------|-----|
| **Complexity overload** | 30+ metrics, 10-page OKR docs, meetings run over | Max 3 goals/quarter, 5-7 metrics/scorecard, "If we could only track 3 things..." |
| **No connection to daily work** | People can't name quarterly priorities; goals only discussed in reviews | Start every meeting referencing a goal; daily huddle: "What am I doing today that moves a Rock?" |
| **Goodhart's Law** (metric gaming) | Sales closes bad-fit deals; marketing inflates lead numbers | Pair quantity with quality metric; separate OKRs from compensation |
| **Leadership doesn't model it** | CEO has no personal OKRs; leadership skips meetings when "busy" | CEO must have visible, personal OKRs; leadership never cancels weekly meeting |
| **All goals, no execution system** | Beautiful docs nobody references; end-of-quarter scramble | Implement full cascade (Quarterly --> Weekly --> Daily); block time for goal work |
| **Never adjusting** | "But that's what we committed to in January" | Monthly: "Are these still the right goals?" 80/20 rule: 80% committed, 20% flexible |

## 6.5 Meta-Metrics -- Measuring the Measurement System

Review these quarterly:

| Meta-Metric | Question | Target | Red Flag |
|-------------|----------|--------|----------|
| **Participation Rate** | What % of people actively use the system? | 90%+ | <70% = system is dying |
| **Goal Awareness** | Can every employee name the top 3 priorities? | 90%+ can | <50% = communication breakdown |
| **Execution Score Trend** | Are execution scores improving, stable, declining? | Stable at 80-90% | Declining 3+ consecutive weeks |
| **Goal Achievement Rate** | What % of quarterly goals are achieved? | 70-80% | <50% = too ambitious or broken execution; >90% = not ambitious enough |
| **Meeting Quality Score** | Do people rate meetings as valuable? | 8+ out of 10 | <7 for 4+ consecutive weeks |
| **Metric Relevance** | Are we measuring the right things? | 0 unused metrics | Any metric not influencing a decision in 90 days = remove it |
| **Time-to-Insight** | How quickly do problems show up in data? | <1 week | Problems discovered through complaints not data |
| **Behavioral Change** | Has measurement changed how people work? | Goals referenced in everyday conversation | Goals only discussed in formal meetings |

## 6.6 30-Day Implementation Roadmap

### Week 1: Foundation

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| 1 | Leadership reads and agrees on approach |
| 2 | Draft or review BHAG and 3-Year Picture |
| 3 | Set 3-5 quarterly Rocks (with owners and success criteria) |
| 4 | Create CEO scorecard (5-7 key metrics) |
| 5 | Schedule the weekly meeting (same day/time forever) |

### Week 2: Team Setup

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| 6 | Each department head creates their scorecard (5-7 metrics) |
| 7 | Each leader sets 1-3 individual Rocks aligned to company Rocks |
| 8 | Create visible scoreboard (physical or digital) |
| 9 | Train team on weekly meeting format |
| 10 | Hold first weekly meeting (expect it to be imperfect) |

### Week 3: Daily Habits

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| 11 | Start daily huddles (10 min, standing, same time) |
| 12 | Each person identifies their lead domino for the week |
| 13 | Introduce daily tracking (15 min morning + 10 min evening) |
| 14 | Each person creates their personal weekly scorecard |
| 15 | Second weekly meeting (should be smoother) |

### Week 4: Refinement

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| 16 | Review what's working and what's not |
| 17 | Adjust scorecards based on first 2 weeks of data |
| 18 | Refine meeting agendas (too long? too short? right topics?) |
| 19 | Celebrate early wins publicly |
| 20 | Commit to the system for the full quarter (minimum viable trial) |

### System Selection by Team Size

| Team Size | Recommended Stack | Skip |
|-----------|-------------------|------|
| **1-5 employees** | 12 Week Year + Lead Domino + Simple Scorecard | Formal OKR cascading, EOS (too heavy) |
| **5-20 employees** | EOS (Rocks + L10 + Scorecard) OR OKRs + Daily Huddle | Full 4DX unless needed for breakthrough |
| **20-50 employees** | EOS or OKRs with full cascade + 4DX for breakthrough initiatives | Nothing; use full cadence |
| **50+ employees** | OKRs with alignment + FAST goal principles + 4DX for cross-functional WIGs | Strict cascading (use alignment instead) |
